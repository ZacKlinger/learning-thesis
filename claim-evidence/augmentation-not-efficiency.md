Thesis-seed v1: *"'Efficiency' in education usually means doing the existing thing faster, which entrenches it. 'Augmentation' means enabling kinds of learning that couldn't happen without the tool. Most edtech sits on the wrong side of this line."*

This is the thread most exposed to steelman risk. Two strong counter-positions need to be engaged early, not late:

1. **Cognitive load theory (Kirschner, Sweller, Clark 2006):** For novices, "ambitious" minimally-guided learning is empirically inferior to direct guided instruction. A lot of "augmentation" rhetoric historically rests on discovery/inquiry framings that this literature attacks at the root.
2. **Mastery-learning efficiency case (Bloom's 2-sigma, contemporary AI tutoring):** Some of the most defensible empirical claims for tech in education *are* efficiency claims — getting more students to mastery of well-defined material in less time. Framing those as a category error may discard the strongest evidentiary ground.

The thread should not advance until both are read and represented.

### Open questions
- What is the cleanest operational definition of "previously impossible learning"? Without one, the augmentation/efficiency distinction collapses into rhetoric.
- Where in the literature has someone tested the *kinds* of learning a tool enables (rather than the same outcome faster)? Candidates: Scardamalia & Bereiter on Knowledge Building; CSILE/Knowledge Forum studies; Papert's Logo evaluations.
- The Logo evaluations are themselves a cautionary tale — the early enthusiastic results did not hold under scrutiny. Read those critiques (Pea & Kurland 1984; subsequent reviews) before leaning on Papert.
- Is "augmentation" a property of the tool or of the pedagogy around it? The same software in two classrooms can be either.
