## Claim: The early-MOOC era's headline enrolment figures were not predictive of completion or of equity-of-access; the median completion rate was 6.5% of enrolees, and completers skewed toward the already-educated.

This is the cleanest concrete instance of the historical pattern this thread
is built on: an optimistic capability claim (free elite-university courses
at planet scale) → modest measured impact on the populations the optimism
named → reabsorption into a more conventional form (certificates,
nanodegrees, professional credentials).

### Supporting evidence
- "The average MOOC course is found to enroll around 43,000 students, 6.5%
  of whom complete the course."
  — [Jordan 2014](../sources/Jordan-2014-mooc-completion-rates.md), abstract, p. 133 — layer: empirical
- "It is misleading to invoke early enrolment and completion figures as
  representative of the phenomenon; six-figure enrolments are atypical, with
  the median average enrolment being 42,844 students, and decreasing over
  time as the number of courses available continues to increase."
  — [Jordan 2014](../sources/Jordan-2014-mooc-completion-rates.md), p. 150 — layer: empirical
- (On Coursera demographics, citing Koller & Ng 2013 and Emanuel 2012/2013):
  "the majority of their students are already educated to at least
  undergraduate degree level, with 42.8% holding a bachelor's degree, and a
  further 36.7% and 5.4% holding master's and doctoral degrees ... The author
  concludes that MOOCs are failing in their goal to reach disadvantaged
  students who would not ordinarily have access to educational opportunities."
  — [Jordan 2014](../sources/Jordan-2014-mooc-completion-rates.md), p. 134 — layer: empirical (Jordan citing secondary sources)

### Counter-evidence (against treating this as proof of "MOOCs failed")
- Jordan herself: "There are potentially many ways in which MOOC students may
  participate in and benefit from courses without completing the assessments."
  — [Jordan 2014](../sources/Jordan-2014-mooc-completion-rates.md), p. 150 — layer: empirical

  *Why this counts as counter-evidence:* completion-as-success is one frame,
  not the only one. A reading of MOOCs that treats 6.5% completion as
  catastrophic failure has to defend why completion is the right metric for
  a free, no-commitment offering. The thesis must not cherry-pick.
- The Coursera-demographics finding is reported from secondary sources
  (Koller & Ng 2013; Emanuel 2012/2013), not from Jordan's own dataset. The
  chain of evidence should be followed before this claim is leaned on heavily.

### Open questions
- The Jordan dataset is 18-months-old as of *her* publication (2014) and
  considerably older now. The MOOC platforms restructured around completion
  incentives after this paper (Coursera Specializations, Udacity Nanodegrees,
  edX MicroMasters). Has the steady-state completion rate moved? Has the
  demographic profile?
- The Pappano 2012 NYT piece is the canonical hype anchor and would let the
  thread pair the hype rhetoric with the reality directly. It is paywalled
  and the fetcher could not retrieve it. Either Zack supplies a clipping, or
  the thread quotes it second-hand via Jordan (who cites it on p. 134).
- The actual base-rate work (Cuban 1986, 2001; Watters 2021) is required
  before this thread can move from "here is one instance of the pattern" to
  "here is the pattern." All three are paywalled. Until Zack obtains them
  the thread has one data point, not a documented pattern.

## Claim (meta-observation): Educational research itself documents the rhetorical pattern this thread is trying to name — but from inside the empirical-side critique of constructivism rather than from outside the institution.

Surfaced from the KSC 2006 reading. Worth recording because it cuts in an
interesting direction: it is a structural argument about *educational reform
rhetoric* made by authors who are themselves making a sharp normative claim
in that same rhetoric.

### Supporting evidence
- "Each new set of advocates for unguided approaches seemed either unaware of
  or uninterested in previous evidence that unguided approaches had not been
  validated. This pattern produced discovery learning, which gave way to
  experiential learning, which gave way to problem-based and inquiry
  learning, which now gives way to constructivist instructional techniques."
  — [Kirschner, Sweller & Clark 2006](../sources/Kirschner-Sweller-Clark-2006-minimal-guidance.md), p. 79 — layer: empirical

### Counter-evidence
- *KSC's framing applies to the rhetoric of constructivist pedagogy
  specifically; it does not establish the broader Cuban/Watters pattern about
  educational technology generally. The two cycles overlap (Papert is a node
  on both) but are not the same cycle. Don't conflate them.*

### Open questions
- The constructivist-pedagogy cycle KSC describe and the
  educational-technology cycle Cuban describes both run through the same
  decade-scale rhythm of enthusiasm and disappointment. Is this the *same*
  phenomenon at different layers (institutional immune response to a class
  of reform), or two distinct phenomena that share a tempo? Reading Cuban
  is the prerequisite for answering this.
- KSC's "this pattern produced discovery → experiential → PBL → constructivist"
  characterisation is itself contested. Hmelo-Silver et al. (2007), not yet
  read, will offer the counter-frame.

## Carry-forward open questions (unchanged from v1)

The first set of questions opened with the thread last week, restated to
keep them in scope:

- **What is the actual pattern Cuban documents?** Cuban is more careful than
  the common citation suggests. Required reading before this thread can do
  heavy work.
- **What is structurally different about generative AI as an educational
  technology?** Honest forms of this argument: (a) conversational artifact
  not fixed curriculum; (b) cost-curve collapsing; (c) bottom-up student
  adoption outside schools. Each is testable; none is established.
- **What is the right unit of analysis?** Cuban's record is mostly about
  classroom practice. If AI is changing learning *outside* schools, the
  historical pattern may not bind.
- **What pattern of evidence would falsify the "history rhymes" frame?**
  If we cannot specify one in advance, the frame is unfalsifiable.
- **Are there counter-examples?** The textbook, the blackboard, the
  calculator, the standardised test booklet — these *are* technologies that
  reshaped schooling. What made them succeed where film, radio, and TV did
  not?
