## Claim (provisional, partial): When a wave of educational technology is deployed at scale into existing school systems with conventional curricula, measurable effects on test scores tend to be null even when access expands dramatically.

This is a one-data-point claim, anchored on the Cristia et al. 2017 OLPC Peru RCT. It cannot bear the weight of the thread's thesis-seed framing alone; promoted to "claim" status only because it is the cleanest large-RCT finding the thread points to and because the abstract is verbatim-verifiable even though the full PDF is unavailable.

### Supporting evidence
- "This paper presents results from a large-scale randomized evaluation of the One Laptop per Child program, using data collected after 15 months of implementation in 318 primary schools in rural Peru. The program increased the ratio of computers per student from 0.12 to 1.18 in treatment schools. This expansion in access translated into substantial increases in use of computers both at school and at home. No evidence is found of effects on test scores in math and language. There is some evidence, though inconclusive, about positive effects on general cognitive skills." — Cristia, Ibarrarán, Cueto, Santiago & Severín 2017, *American Economic Journal: Applied Economics* 9(3), pp. 295–320, **abstract** verbatim from aeaweb.org landing page, accessed 2026-05-12 — layer: empirical. **Caveat:** abstract only; the full article is paywalled. Recorded in `leads/tech-cycles-in-education-leads.md` pending mirror.

### Cross-listed visionary framing (not yet evidence for the historical-pattern claim, but relevant to its interpretation)
- "Schools are fundamentally alike in all countries, be they fascist, democratic or socialist, big or small, rich or poor. This identity of the school system forces us to recognize the profound world-wide identity of myth, mode of production, and method of social control, despite the great variety of mythologies in which the myth finds expression." — [Illich 1971](../sources/Illich-1971-deschooling-society.md), Ch. 6, p. 32 — layer: visionary. (Illich's claim explains *why* the historical pattern Cuban documents is so consistent: the institution's hidden curriculum is invariant across ideologies and technologies, and absorbs them.)
- "Technology is available to develop either independence and learning or bureaucracy and teaching." — [Illich 1971](../sources/Illich-1971-deschooling-society.md), Ch. 6, p. 34 — layer: visionary. (The visionary frame for what an optimistic AI-in-education case would have to look like to break the cycle: deploy the technology toward independence rather than toward extending the bureaucracy.)
- "Deschooling, which we cannot stop, could mean the advent of a 'brave new world' dominated by well-intentioned administrators of programmed instruction." — [Illich 1971](../sources/Illich-1971-deschooling-society.md), Ch. 6, p. 44 — layer: visionary. (The pessimistic case for AI tutoring, written 53 years early. The thread cannot ignore this anticipation.)

### Open questions

The first set of questions, ordered roughly by how load-bearing each is for the thesis.

- **What is the actual pattern Cuban documents?** Many people cite Cuban as having shown "tech doesn't change schools." Read first; the actual claim is more specific and more interesting. Particularly: which features of an innovation predict its absorption vs. rejection by the existing grammar of schooling?
- **What is structurally different about generative AI as an educational technology?** The honest forms of this argument: (a) the user-facing artifact is a conversational partner rather than a fixed curriculum, so the medium does not require institutional adoption; (b) the cost curve is collapsing in a way prior waves did not; (c) the technology is being adopted by students directly outside schools, bottom-up rather than top-down. Each of these claims is testable; none is established.
- **What is the right unit of analysis?** Cuban's record is mostly about *classroom practice*. If AI is changing learning *outside* schools (kids using tutors at home, adult learners on their own), the historical pattern may not apply because the institution is being routed around rather than reformed.
- **What pattern of evidence would falsify the "history rhymes" frame?** If we cannot specify one in advance, the frame is unfalsifiable and should not be used as a load-bearing pillar.
- **MOOCs are the most recent test case.** What did the MOOC era actually demonstrate — about adoption, about completion, about the gap between hype and outcome — and what is the right lesson to carry forward into AI-tutoring claims?
- **Are there counter-examples?** Calculators, the move from oral to written examinations, the textbook itself: each is a technology that *did* change schooling. What was different about those?
- **What does the personalized-learning lineage (Pressey → Skinner → CAI → adaptive-learning platforms → AI tutors), as Watters tells it, predict about adaptive-AI hype today? Is the pattern the same or has something genuinely changed?
