### Open questions
- What is the actual history of why American schools took their current shape? The Committee of Ten (1893), the Cardinal Principles (1918), the Conant report (1959) — each pushed in a different direction. The "frozen since 1900" narrative does not survive contact with the record.
- Does "ambitious learning" produce measurable gains, or do its strongest evaluations rely on outcomes (engagement, agency, identity) that are valuable but harder to defend as comparable to test-score evidence?
- Sizer's *Horace's Compromise* and Mehta & Fine's *In Search of Deeper Learning* both document how rare ambition is in practice — including in schools that explicitly aim for it. Why? Is the failure mode pedagogical, structural, or about adult labor conditions?
- What is gained, and what is lost, in framing the existing shape of school as "compromise" vs. as a stable equilibrium with its own logic?
