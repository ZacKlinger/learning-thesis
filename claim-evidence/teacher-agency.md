# Thread: Teacher agency

Thesis-seed v1: *"Scripted curricula and tools-as-replacement are eroding the conditions for great teaching. Any AI-in-education vision that further reduces teacher judgment is a category error."*

**Status (2026-05-30):** Per the 2026-05-27 steer, this thread is being
reframed. The load-bearing claim is no longer "teacher agency is being
stripped" framed as teacher-as-vulnerable. It is **workload-constraint-on-
the-system**: the system imposes conditions (teaching to the middle of 30,
scripted curricula, administrative burden) that make great teaching
impossible regardless of the teacher's skill or intent. AI's role in this
thread is *not* to route around the teacher. It is to remove the workload
constraints — to give teachers back the time, attention, and personalization-
bandwidth the system has been taking from them. The teacher is not the
bottleneck; the system is. The steer is explicit: framings in prior writing
that treated teachers as bottlenecks are to be corrected as they surface.

Two verified entries on the ledger as of this Saturday: Dewey 1897 (the
visionary anchor — teacher as "member of the community" selecting
influences) and Cuban 2024 (a contemporary statement — teaching is complex,
improvisational, emotional, and AI advocates routinely fail to understand
this). The empirical counters that would balance the thread (Bryk et al.
2010 on workload as the load-bearing variable; Lemov 2010 on the
structured-teaching tradition; the Direct Instruction record; the "floor
effect" of scripting on under-resourced classrooms) have not yet been read.
Illich (1971) is cited as a *prior question* that the thread implicitly
accepts an answer to.

---

## Claim: In a tradition descended from Dewey, the teacher's role is not to impose ideas or content but to select and shape the social influences that act on the child — a description that is incompatible with both the scripted-curriculum tradition and the AI-as-substitute-teacher frame.

This is the visionary anchor for the thesis seed. Its evidentiary status is
*framing*, not causal. The strongest empirical counters — that scripted
curricula raise the floor for novice teachers, that Direct Instruction
outperformed alternatives in Project Follow Through — are not yet in
the ledger.

### Supporting evidence
- "I believe that the teacher's place and work in the school is to be
  interpreted from this same basis. The teacher is not in the school to impose
  certain ideas or to form certain habits in the child, but is there as a
  member of the community to select the influences which shall affect the
  child and to assist him in properly responding to these influences."
  — [Dewey 1897](../sources/Dewey-1897-pedagogic-creed.md), Article Two, ¶20 — layer: visionary
- "I believe that the teacher's business is simply to determine on the basis
  of larger experience and riper wisdom, how the discipline of life shall come
  to the child."
  — [Dewey 1897](../sources/Dewey-1897-pedagogic-creed.md), Article Two, ¶22 — layer: visionary
- "I believe that every teacher should realize the dignity of his calling;
  that he is a social servant set apart for the maintenance of proper social
  order and the securing of the right social growth."
  — [Dewey 1897](../sources/Dewey-1897-pedagogic-creed.md), Article Five, ¶72 — layer: visionary
- "I believe that under existing conditions far too much of the stimulus and
  control proceeds from the teacher, because of neglect of the idea of the
  school as a form of social life."
  — [Dewey 1897](../sources/Dewey-1897-pedagogic-creed.md), Article Two, ¶19 — layer: visionary

  *Note:* this last quote is *not* a brief against teacher agency — it is a
  brief against the teacher as the *sole* source of stimulus, in favour of the
  broader community life of the classroom. Read against the contemporary
  AI-as-tutor frame, Dewey's argument is closer to "the social fabric of the
  classroom is the educational instrument" than to "automate the teacher
  away." The teacher-agency thread should not collapse this distinction.

### Counter-evidence (against the thesis seed)
- *(none yet read in the verified sense — but Lemov 2010, the Direct
  Instruction tradition, and the Project Follow Through record are the three
  empirically strongest counters that must enter before the thread can do
  heavy work. Leads in `leads/teacher-agency-leads.md`.)*

### Supporting evidence (a contemporary articulation, separate from Dewey's framing)
- "Teaching, then, is not a mechanical act of connecting dots. Teaching is
  a complex act that requires knowledge of subject matter, managerial
  skills, and emotional labor. Often, it is improvisational. Most
  important, however, is that teaching requires gaining students' trust.
  It is both an art and a science that takes years to master."
  — [Cuban 2024](../sources/Cuban-2024-will-ai-transform.md) — layer: practitioner-historian (blog)
- "So I have concluded that those pushing AI use in classrooms fail to
  understand the complexity of teaching."
  — [Cuban 2024](../sources/Cuban-2024-will-ai-transform.md) — layer: practitioner-historian (blog)

  *Why this is here:* Cuban's "complexity of teaching" claim agrees with
  the thread's load-bearing point — that automating "teaching" the way
  edtech rhetoric describes is a category error — but from a different
  angle than Dewey. Dewey's claim is structural (the teacher is part of
  the social fabric of the classroom); Cuban's claim is phenomenological
  (the teacher is doing improvisational emotional labor that AI
  characterizations omit). Read together, they triangulate a position
  that is *not* "teacher agency is being stripped by AI" but
  "AI-as-substitute-teacher mis-describes what teachers actually do."
  Worth distinguishing: this thread is compatible with AI being a
  powerful tool *for* teachers; it is incompatible with AI being a
  *substitute for* them.

### Counter-counter (a prior position the seed implicitly rejects)
- "Universal education through schooling is not feasible. ... Neither new
  attitudes of teachers toward their pupils nor the proliferation of
  educational hardware or software (in classroom or bedroom)... will deliver
  universal education."
  — [Illich 1971](../sources/Illich-1971-deschooling-society.md), Introduction — layer: visionary

  *Why this is here:* Illich would say the entire "protect teacher agency"
  debate operates inside an institutional form that itself manufactures the
  dependencies it claims to remedy. The thesis does not have to accept this,
  but it has to engage it rather than assume the school as a given.

### Open questions
- The thread's load-bearing variable is now **workload constraint**, not
  agency-in-the-abstract. The empirical question is: how much of what
  Cuban calls "the complexity of teaching" is intrinsic, and how much is
  artefactual workload (class size, administrative tasks, prep time,
  data-entry mandates) the system has imposed? Bryk et al. (2010) is the
  most-cited source on this; the wishlist now points at it.
- "Teacher agency" is probably three constructs, not one — autonomy over
  *content*, over *method*, over *pacing*. The empirical literature on each
  may not behave the same. (Open in v1; restated.)
- What is the empirically strongest evidence that scripted curricula raise
  the floor for under-prepared teachers (Lemov, Direct Instruction, Reading
  First era)? Does that floor come at a ceiling cost? — and how does the
  workload-constraint reframing change this question? (If scripting is
  workload-relief disguised as content-control, the empirical record
  may be conflating two different things.)
- Distinguish "teacher agency" from "teacher discretion to do harm." The
  strongest counter to the seed is that some agency-removing reforms produce
  real gains for the lowest-served students. This is the hardest counter and
  Zack should write through it, not around it.
- Dewey's "teacher as member of the community" is a *social* description, not
  a *judgmental* one. Translated to AI-in-education: the right question is
  not "what tasks should AI take from the teacher" but "does the AI become a
  member of the classroom community, or does it route around it?"
- Per the steer: the thread should treat *teacher-as-bottleneck* as a
  framing the thesis explicitly rejects. When sources use that framing
  (some AI-tutoring papers will), surface it as a position the thesis
  argues against rather than absorbing it.
