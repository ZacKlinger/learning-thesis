## Thesis

A meta-aggregation of publicly available enrolment and completion data for
the early generation of MOOCs (Coursera, EdX, Udacity, courses through July
2013; n=91 courses with enrolment data, n=39 with completion data). Jordan
finds that the early headline numbers (the Stanford AI course, 160,000
enrolled / 20,000 completing) are atypical; the median MOOC enrolment is
~43,000 and the median completion rate is 6.5% of enrollees (9.8% of "active"
students, where active = at least one login / quiz / video). Completion rates
are *consistent* across time, university ranking, and total enrolment, and
*negatively correlated* with course length. The paper does not editorialise
the optimistic-hype frame, but its numbers are the most-cited counter to it.

## Key quoted claims

- "The average MOOC course is found to enroll around 43,000 students, 6.5%
  of whom complete the course." — abstract, p. 133
- "Six-figure enrolment statistics have generated a good deal of interest in
  MOOCs in the higher education sector, and are frequently conflated with
  active participation or completion. However, the earliest courses are the
  most frequently cited examples and may not be representative of how the
  phenomenon is developing, and the extent to which enrolment numbers are
  indicative of completion has not been explored comprehensively." — p. 135
- "Completion rates range from 0.9% to 36.1%, with a median value of 6.5%
  (Figure 4). The data is skewed, so the higher completion rates are not
  representative, with completion rates of 5% being typical." — p. 147
- "No significant relationships were found between completion rate and date,
  university ranking, or the total number of students enrolled. Completion
  rates remained consistent across these factors." — p. 147
- "It is misleading to invoke early enrolment and completion figures as
  representative of the phenomenon; six-figure enrolments are atypical, with
  the median average enrolment being 42,844 students, and decreasing over time
  as the number of courses available continues to increase." — p. 150
- "The majority of courses have been found to have completion rates of less
  than 10% of those who enroll, with a median average of 6.5%." — p. 150
- (On Coursera demographics, citing Koller & Ng 2013 and Emanuel 2012/2013):
  "the majority of their students are already educated to at least
  undergraduate degree level, with 42.8% holding a bachelor's degree, and a
  further 36.7% and 5.4% holding master's and doctoral degrees ... The author
  concludes that MOOCs are failing in their goal to reach disadvantaged
  students who would not ordinarily have access to educational opportunities."
  — p. 134

## Methods (if empirical)

Aggregation, not RCT. Public-domain data scraped from class-central.com,
university reports, news stories, MOOC student blogs, and crowdsourced
submissions to the author's blog (Jordan 2013). Coverage: 91 courses with
enrolment data (32.6% of the 279 completed Coursera/EdX/Udacity courses at
the cut-off), 39 with completion data (15.1%). Statistical methods:
regression after Box-Cox transformation; correlations reported with R² and
p-values. *Limitations Jordan herself acknowledges*: (i) selection bias
toward courses that publicly released data; (ii) "completion" is defined as
earning a certificate, which obscures other forms of meaningful participation;
(iii) student voice is absent from the dataset.

## Where this sits in the canon

- The cleanest empirical anchor in the **tech-cycles in education** thread
  for the MOOC-era reality check. Pairs with Pappano (2012), "The Year of the
  MOOC" — the *NYT* hype piece — which has not yet been retrieved (NYT URL
  failed; demoted to leads).
- The pattern Jordan documents (median completion ~6.5%, demographics skewed
  toward the already-educated) is the most concrete recent instance of the
  Cuban-style cycle: substantial enrolment, modest completion, weak evidence
  for the equity claims that motivated the optimism.
- Cited in `claim-evidence/tech-cycles-in-education.md` as supporting evidence
  for the claim that the most recent prior wave's adoption pathway matched
  the historical pattern more closely than its early advocates predicted.

## Tensions / contradictions

- **Completion is not the right metric for all MOOC participants.** Jordan is
  explicit about this (p. 150). A large share of MOOC enrollees are professional
  learners sampling for utility, not pursuing certification. Citing 6.5%
  completion as "failure" without engaging this complication is rhetorically
  cheap. The thesis should not.
- **The data is 18 months old as of publication.** Some platforms — notably
  Coursera's professional certificate / specialization track and Udacity's
  nanodegrees — restructured around completion incentives after this paper.
  The early-MOOC pattern Jordan documents is not necessarily the steady-state
  pattern.
- **Active-student completion is 9.8%, not 6.5%.** When restricted to students
  who logged in at least once, the median is meaningfully higher. The frame
  "MOOCs barely worked" tilts the number toward the lower end; the frame "of
  people who started, ~10% finished" is also defensible from the same data.
- **The equity finding (skewed toward already-educated) is the strongest one
  for the thesis.** It is a category Jordan reports from secondary sources
  (Koller & Ng 2013; Emanuel 2012/2013), not from her own data; the
  evidentiary chain should be followed before this claim is leaned on.
