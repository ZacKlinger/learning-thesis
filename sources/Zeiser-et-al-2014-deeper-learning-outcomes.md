## Thesis

The third and final report of the AIR / Hewlett Foundation *Study of Deeper
Learning: Opportunities and Outcomes* — a quasi-experimental evaluation of
ten high school networks that explicitly pursue "deeper learning" (Asia
Society International Studies, Big Picture Learning, ConnectEd,
EdVisions Schools, Envision Schools, Expeditionary Learning, High Tech High,
Internationals Network for Public Schools, New Tech Network, New Visions for
Public Schools). The study matched 15 "network" high schools in California
and New York with 12 "non-network" comparison schools (13–15 pairs after
combining/sharing), gathered four cohorts of incoming-Grade-9 student data
(2007–08 through 2010–11), and used propensity-score-weighted doubly-robust
regression to estimate effects on (1) cognitive competency (OECD PISA-Based
Test for Schools and state ELA/math tests); (2) interpersonal and
intrapersonal competencies (eight survey-measured constructs); (3) on-time
high school graduation; (4) postsecondary enrollment and persistence (via
the National Student Clearinghouse).

Headline findings: students at network schools scored 0.11–0.12 SD higher on
PBTS subjects (reading, math, science), 0.10 SD higher on state math, 0.05 SD
higher on state ELA. They reported significantly higher collaboration skills
(0.20 SD), academic engagement (0.20 SD), motivation to learn (0.12 SD), and
self-efficacy (0.13 SD). No significant differences on creative thinking,
perseverance, locus of control, or self-management. On-time four-year
graduation was 9 percentage points higher (65% vs. 56%). Overall postsecondary
enrollment was *not* different, but enrollment in four-year and selective
institutions was higher for network students. Effects were similar for
students entering with low vs. high prior achievement — the equity finding
the deeper-learning advocates hoped for.

For the thesis, this is the **at-scale empirical anchor for the
ambition-over-complacency thread** and a real (not anecdotal) data point that
schools organised around ambitious deeper-learning practices can produce
better outcomes for students of all entering-achievement levels — without
sacrificing the standardised-test outcomes the skeptics insist on. It is
also, however, a study whose authors are unusually honest about its limits:
schools were *selected* to be at least moderately well-implementing (no
generalisation to a random sample); effects varied substantially across
school pairs (some null, some positive, one with consistent negative
intrapersonal effects); the design is quasi-experimental, not RCT; and the
magnitude is *comparable to other rigorously evaluated reform efforts*
(Talent Development High Schools, Early College, Expeditionary Learning
middle schools) — i.e., this is a real effect of useful magnitude, but not a
revolutionary one.

The thread should not over-claim. The result here is roughly: when you run a
high school deliberately around deeper-learning practices, with at least
moderate fidelity, you produce a 0.10–0.20 SD bump on cognitive measures,
a 0.20 SD bump on engagement-and-collaboration measures, and 9 percentage
points more on-time graduation. That is real and contemporary and at-scale.
It is also the kind of effect-size that history-rhymes-skeptics (Watters,
Cuban) would correctly point out has been claimed by many prior reforms.

## Key quoted claims

- (Headline cognitive finding:) "Students attending network schools scored
  higher in all three subjects tested (reading, mathematics, and science)
  with effect sizes of 0.12, 0.11, and 0.11 of a standard deviation,
  respectively."
  — p. 14
- (Context for the magnitude:) "The results are similar in magnitude to the
  effects obtained for other reforms that have been rigorously evaluated
  and have shown positive effects. For example, a quasi-experimental study
  of Talent Development High Schools found an impact of 0.12 standard
  deviations on mathematics achievement (Kemple, Herlihy, & Smith, 2005)
  and a randomized study of Early College High Schools found an impact of
  0.14 standard deviations on high school ELA achievement (Berger et al.,
  2013)."
  — p. 15
- (Honest between-school variation:) "The estimated effect of attending a
  network school on PBTS reading, mathematics, and science scores was
  positive and significant in some pairs, and not significant in others.
  There were no significant negative effects."
  — p. 15
- (Equity finding for cognitive outcomes:) "The effects of attending a
  network school on PBTS scores did not differ among students who entered
  high school with lower and higher prior achievement. ... The results
  suggest that both students who entered high school with below-average
  scores and students who entered with above-average scores benefited from
  attending a network school."
  — p. 17
- (Interpersonal/intrapersonal findings:) "Students who attended
  participating network high schools reported greater collaboration skills
  and higher levels of academic engagement, motivation to learn, and
  self-efficacy than similar students who attended non-network high
  schools. There were no significant differences between network and
  non-network students on reported creative thinking, perseverance, locus
  of control, or self-management."
  — p. 18
- (Graduation finding:) "approximately 65 percent of students who attended
  participating network high schools graduated within four years from a
  high school in the same district. For similar students who attended
  non-network high schools, 56 percent were estimated to graduate within
  four years from a school in the same district. ... attending a network
  school increased the on-time graduation rate by approximately 9
  percentage points."
  — pp. 20–21
- (Postsecondary nuance:) "Attending a network school did not affect the
  probability that students enrolled in a postsecondary institution,
  enrolled in a two-year institution, or persisted to the second year of
  postsecondary education by fall 2013. ... Although attending a network
  school did not affect overall college attendance rates, students who
  attended participating network high schools were more likely to enroll
  in four-year institutions than were students who attended non-network
  high schools. Students who attended network schools were also more
  likely to enroll in selective institutions."
  — pp. 23–24
- (The mechanism claim from the discussion:) "Taken together, these results
  suggest that the key mechanisms underlying the positive effects of
  attending a network school reported in this study are the learning
  opportunities enacted within the schools—in particular, opportunities to
  engage in complex problem solving and creative thinking; opportunities
  to communicate, collaborate, learn how to learn, and receive feedback;
  and opportunities for assessments aligned with deeper learning,
  interdisciplinary learning, and making real-world connections."
  — pp. 27–28
- (Authorial honesty about generalisation:) "because we chose schools that
  were implementing networks' approaches to fostering deeper learning at
  least moderately well, we do not know what results would have been
  obtained had we sampled network schools that implemented network
  approaches with lower levels of fidelity."
  — p. 28
- (Authorial honesty about between-pair variation:) "our results showed
  substantial variation across pairs in the effects of attending network
  schools. Although we have documented this variation across schools, we
  do not have the evidence required to explain this variation."
  — p. 28

## Methods

Quasi-experimental matched-pair design. Fifteen network high schools matched
with twelve non-network comparison schools (3 non-network used twice → 15
pairs); schools selected purposively from California and New York City, where
the participating networks operate at scale; matching done via propensity-
score weighting on Grade 8 prior achievement and demographic covariates;
analysis via doubly-robust regression (combines propensity weighting with
covariate-adjusted regression to reduce reliance on either correctly
specifying the propensity model or the outcome model alone — provides valid
estimates if *either* is correctly specified, per Funk et al. 2011).

Four cohorts of incoming Grade 9 students (2007–08 through 2010–11), with
total samples of ~1,267 PBTS-completers, ~1,762 survey-completers,
~13,831 students for high school graduation analysis, and ~11,165 for
postsecondary analysis (via NSC). Outcomes: OECD PBTS (administered for
this study by CTB McGraw Hill); California CAHSEE / New York Comprehensive
English and Integrated Algebra Regents; 8 survey-measured constructs;
on-time graduation; NSC postsecondary enrollment / persistence.

Methodological limitations the authors name and the thesis should carry:
(i) selection of moderately-or-highly-implementing schools — not
generalisable to a random sample of network schools; (ii) substantial
between-pair variation that the design lacks power to attribute to specific
school characteristics; (iii) the design counts cross-district transfers as
non-graduates, depressing reported graduation rates relative to district
reporting (sensitivity analyses showed smaller but similarly significant
positive effects); (iv) self-reported survey measures for intrapersonal /
interpersonal outcomes (the standard limitation); (v) one school pair drove
much of the negative-result mass on creative thinking — sensitivity analysis
without that pair changed several null results to significant positives.

## Where this sits in the canon

- **The contemporary at-scale empirical anchor for the ambition-over-
  complacency thread.** Cuban 2024 / 2025 and Watters 2018 carry the
  historical-skeptic side; Zeiser 2014 carries the "here is real positive
  evidence from a well-implemented contemporary practice at scale" side.
  The honest read of the ledger now has both.
- **A second-order test of HSDC 2007's bounding move.** HSDC argued that
  PBL / inquiry-as-actually-practiced is heavily scaffolded and that the
  evidence for it (Geier, Lynch, Dochy) is real. Zeiser is a different
  generation of evidence — broader, focused on networks rather than
  individual programs, contemporary — that runs in the same direction.
  The augmentation thread now has *both* the HSDC scaffolded-inquiry
  evidence and the Zeiser network-level effectiveness evidence.
- **A complement, not a substitute, for Scardamalia & Bereiter 1994.**
  S&B describe the design and mechanism; Zeiser shows that schools built
  around closely-related practices (PBL, internships, collaborative work,
  portfolios) produce measurable outcomes at scale. The mechanism / outcome
  pairing is now on the ledger for the augmentation thread.
- Relates to: HSDC 2007 (the scaffolded-inquiry case Zeiser tests at
  scale); Scardamalia & Bereiter 1994 (the design-theoretical case Zeiser
  partially operationalises); Mehta & Fine 2019 (the qualitative
  companion — ethnographic data on how rare deep learning actually is in
  US high schools; not yet on the ledger); Bryk et al. 2010 (essential
  supports framework, adjacent unit-of-analysis).

## Tensions / contradictions

- **The Watters / Cuban historical-skeptic challenge.** Zeiser shows the
  deeper-learning networks work for *the students who attend them*, at
  the schools that *implement the model at least moderately well*, in
  the time window of the study. Watters would correctly point out that
  this is the typical shape of an optimistic education-reform finding:
  "the schools doing the new thing well got better results than the
  schools not doing it." Cuban would correctly point out that the
  history of school reform is full of programmes that produced
  Zeiser-magnitude effects in evaluations and were nonetheless absorbed
  back into the grammar of schooling within a decade. The thread
  should record Zeiser as positive evidence *for the steer's framing*
  ("technology / pedagogy can enable better learning") and as something
  that does not by itself answer the adoption-pathway question
  (whether the deeper-learning network model scales beyond its
  current ~15-school footprint to typical American high schools).
- **Effect-size honesty.** 0.10–0.20 SD is real but not transformative.
  An honest essay should not present these as "deeper learning works
  enormously well" — it should present them as "deeper learning works
  about as well as the better-evaluated structural reform efforts of
  the past two decades, with the equity-of-effect bonus that those
  reforms often did not show." The KSC tradition would note that
  this effect-size is in the same range as well-designed direct-
  instruction interventions; the thesis should engage that, not
  brush past it.
- **The "moderately well implemented" filter.** This is the most
  important caveat in the report. The networks self-identified schools
  that met implementation criteria; the comparison schools did not.
  The honest read is that the study answers "does deeper learning, when
  implemented at least moderately well, produce better outcomes?" — not
  "if a typical American high school adopted these practices, would it
  see these results?" The latter question is the one Cuban's century of
  historical evidence pushes against, and Zeiser does not answer it.
- **The variation across pairs.** Some network schools produced strong
  positive effects; others produced null effects; one produced
  consistent negative intrapersonal effects. The mechanism behind this
  variation is unknown. This is the kind of finding that gets
  discussed away as "implementation fidelity" but is actually the most
  scientifically interesting result in the report — and the one that
  most needs follow-up research.
- **The four-year-college finding's interpretation problem.** Zeiser
  notes two possible readings: (a) network schools reduce
  "undermatching" by encouraging students to apply to colleges that
  match their capabilities; (b) network schools produce "overmatching"
  that will lead to lower long-run persistence. The data do not
  distinguish these, and persistence-to-second-year was unchanged
  overall. The thesis should not lean on the four-year-college finding
  without engaging both readings.
- **vs. the thesis's 1:1 AI tutoring framing.** Zeiser's mechanism
  claim is about *opportunities to engage in complex problem solving,
  collaborate, learn how to learn, and receive feedback in
  interdisciplinary, real-world contexts.* This is the Scardamalia &
  Bereiter / HSDC family of practice, not the 1:1 tutoring family. The
  augmentation thread should be careful: the contemporary empirical
  support is for *deeper-learning practices*, not for *AI-tutoring*.
  Generalising from one to the other is the move the thesis needs to
  defend explicitly.
