## Thesis

This is the *slide deck* from the January 2010 CCSR symposium accompanying the
book *Organizing Schools for Improvement: Lessons from Chicago* (University of
Chicago Press, 2010). It is not the book itself. It is a 5-page summary of the
study's headline findings: a 15-year longitudinal study of 100+ Chicago
elementary schools that identified **five "Essential Supports"** as
distinguishing schools that substantially improved in reading and math from
those that stagnated, and that quantified the relationship between each
support's strength and the likelihood of substantial improvement.

The five Essential Supports the slides name: (1) school leadership, (2) parent
community ties, (3) professional capacity, (4) student-centered learning
climate, (5) instructional guidance (curriculum alignment). The headline
empirical claim — read off the bar charts the deck reports — is that schools
strong on these supports were roughly 4–5× as likely to substantially improve
in reading as schools weak on them, and that schools with weak learning climate
plus weak instruction did not improve at all even when other supports were
present. The deck also presents the framework's interaction with community
context: bonding/bridging social capital and rates of student abuse/neglect in
the surrounding neighborhood influenced the likelihood that strong essential
supports could be assembled in the first place.

For the thesis, this is *partial* empirical material. The supports framework
is the load-bearing finding the symposium presents, and it supports a
*structural-conditions* reading of what makes great teaching possible —
adjacent to but not identical with the workload-constraint reframing the
2026-05-27 steer asks the teacher-agency thread to adopt. The slide deck does
not make the workload-on-teachers argument directly; it makes the
essential-supports-on-schools argument, with collaboration and program
coherence and parent-trust load-bearing. The full book (paywalled, not on the
ledger) is reportedly more explicit on workload, but this deck is not.

## Methods

The slides reference a 15-year longitudinal study of Chicago Public Schools
elementary schools, using value-added measures of student achievement growth
(1990–2005) regressed on teacher- and principal-survey measures of the five
essential supports. The asterisks reported in the value-added effect-size
table follow the conventional p<.05/.01/.001 thresholds (the slides use the
\*/\*\*/\*\*\* convention without an explicit key, which is standard for
this audience). The underlying methods are described in the full book and in
a series of CCSR research reports; the deck does not include them.

The deck is *summary* material — its evidentiary weight is the weight of
the underlying CCSR studies it reports on, mediated by the loss of method
detail. Treat as: directionally accurate on the headline finding (essential
supports matter, with large likelihood-of-improvement gaps between strong
and weak schools), but not sufficient on its own to evaluate the design.

## Where this sits in the canon

- The canonical empirical landing place for the *structural-conditions*
  reading of school improvement. The five supports map roughly onto the
  conditions great teaching depends on, which is why the teacher-agency
  thread had Bryk as its load-bearing missing source.
- Adjacent to but distinct from the *workload-on-teachers* framing the
  2026-05-27 steer asks the thread to use. The deck's "professional
  capacity" support includes collective responsibility, innovation
  orientation, and reflective dialogue — these are *capacities*, not
  workload conditions per se. The thread's reframing will need to be
  careful: "workload" is one of several supporting conditions in Bryk's
  framework, not the master variable.
- Relates to: Cuban 2024/2025 (the practitioner-historian register on
  what makes great teaching possible); Lemov 2010 and the Direct
  Instruction tradition (leads — counter-position on
  structured-teaching-as-floor); Sizer 1984 (visionary precursor to the
  "structural conditions" framing).

## Tensions / contradictions

- **vs. the steer's workload-constraint reframing.** The steer's framing
  ("the system imposes workload constraints that make great teaching
  impossible") treats workload as the master variable. Bryk's framework
  has five supports, not one — and within "professional capacity,"
  workload is implicit (in "reflective dialogue" requiring time) rather
  than explicit. The deck does *not* validate the workload-only framing;
  it validates a richer essential-supports framing. The teacher-agency
  thread should record the supports framework as evidence and treat the
  workload reframing as a *subset* claim (workload-relief contributes to
  professional capacity), not the whole story.
- **vs. AI-as-substitute framings.** Bryk's finding that schools with weak
  learning climate plus weak instruction did not improve attendance even
  when other supports were present cuts against AI-as-fix-for-isolated-
  problems framings. Improvement is a *constellation*, not a single
  variable to optimize. AI deployments that improve instruction without
  addressing climate (or vice versa) should not be expected to produce
  the headline effects the deck reports.
- **Source-type caveat.** This is a slide deck. Effect sizes are reported
  with three significant figures and asterisks, but the underlying
  models, controls, and sample-size specifications are not in the
  document. Anything load-bearing should be confirmed against the full
  book or the underlying CCSR reports before being cited in the public
  essay.
