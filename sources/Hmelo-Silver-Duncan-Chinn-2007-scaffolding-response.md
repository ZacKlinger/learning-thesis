## Thesis

The published rejoinder to Kirschner, Sweller & Clark (2006). HSDC argue that
KSC's case is built on two flaws. First, a *pedagogical* flaw: KSC conflate
problem-based learning (PBL) and inquiry learning (IL) with unguided
discovery learning, when in fact PBL and IL as actually practiced are
extensively scaffolded — through expert modeling, just-in-time direct
instruction, tool-imposed structure (Model-It's plan/build/test modes;
GenScope's linked representations; WISE's embedded hints; anchored
instruction's whiteboards), and curriculum-level constraints. The forms of
guidance present in serious PBL/IL "seem to us to be indistinguishable from
some of the forms of guidance recommended by Cognitive Load theorists."
Second, an *evidentiary* flaw: KSC selectively cite older meta-analyses
(Albanese & Mitchell 1993; Berkson 1993) and overlook newer favorable ones
(Vernon & Blake 1993; Dochy et al. 2003) and large-scale quasi-experimental
work (Geier et al.; Lynch et al. 2005; Hickey et al. 1999) showing
significant gains from inquiry-based instruction, including for historically
disadvantaged students. HSDC also reframe the question: "Does it work?" is
the wrong question; the right questions concern under what conditions, for
what outcomes, with what kinds of scaffolding, for which populations.

## Key quoted claims

- "Kirschner and colleagues have indiscriminately lumped together several
  distinct pedagogical approaches—constructivist, discovery, problem-based,
  experiential, and inquiry-based—under the category of minimally guided
  instruction. We argue here that at least some of these approaches, in
  particular, problem-based learning (PBL) and inquiry learning (IL), are
  not minimally guided instructional approaches but rather provide
  extensive scaffolding and guidance to facilitate student learning."
  — p. 99
- "We agree with Kirschner et al. (2006) that there is little evidence to
  suggest that unguided and experientially-based approaches foster
  learning. However, IL and PBL are not discovery approaches and are not
  instances of minimally guided instruction, contrary to the claims of
  Kirschner et al."
  — p. 100
- "In summary, many of the types of scaffolding described provide very
  strong forms of guidance that seem to us to be indistinguishable from
  some of the forms of guidance recommended by Cognitive Load theorists."
  — p. 102
- "Studies showing that unguided or minimally guided instruction is
  inferior to direct instruction are simply irrelevant to most approaches
  implementing PBL or IL."
  — p. 102
- "In a more recent meta-analysis of the effects of PBL, Dochy, Segers,
  Van den Bossche, and Gijbels (2003) found there was no effect of PBL on
  declarative knowledge tests, but studies that compared PBL students with
  those in traditional curricula on measures of knowledge application
  showed a moderate effect size favoring PBL students."
  — p. 103
- "African-American boys in the inquiry classrooms 'caught up' to and
  showed no statistically significant difference from girls after exposure
  to at least one inquiry-based unit." (re: Geier et al. study of
  ~19,000 middle-school students)
  — p. 104
- "Even in this limited review of research on PBL and IL, it is clear that
  the claim that PBL and IL 'does not work' is not well supported, and, in
  fact, there is support for the alternative. But we would argue that 'Does
  it work?' is the wrong question."
  — p. 105
- "While we are not arguing against various forms of direct and more
  heavily guided instruction, of the sort that Kirschner et al advocate, it
  is still unclear how to balance IL and PBL (which are more constructivist
  and experiential) with direct instructional guidance."
  — p. 106

## Methods (if empirical)

Not an empirical paper itself; a theoretical-and-evidentiary response. The
evidence adduced is (i) descriptions of scaffolded inquiry environments
(WISE, GenScope, Model-It, Animal Landlord, anchored instruction / Jasper,
STELLAR PBL, BGuILE) and the forms of guidance they impose; (ii)
meta-analyses HSDC argue KSC overlooked (Vernon & Blake 1993; Dochy, Segers,
Van den Bossche & Gijbels 2003); (iii) large-scale quasi-experimental
classroom studies (Hickey et al. 1999 — 381 GenScope vs. 107 comparison
students; Geier et al. — ~19,000 students across two cohorts in 18 urban
schools with reported effect sizes 0.37–0.44; Lynch et al. 2005 — >2,000
8th-graders in 10 schools); (iv) controlled crossover studies (Capon & Kuhn
2004 on MBA students; Schwartz & Martin 2004 on 9th-grade statistics
instruction). No new data are reported in this paper.

## Tensions / contradictions

- **vs. KSC 2006.** Direct, named collision. HSDC argue KSC's evidentiary
  case is bounded narrowly to *unguided* discovery learning (which HSDC
  also reject) and is "simply irrelevant" to actual PBL/IL practice, which
  is heavily scaffolded. HSDC's strongest move is to make this scaffolding
  concrete (GenScope's linked representations, Model-It's plan/build/test
  staging, anchored-instruction whiteboards, expert modeling, just-in-time
  direct instruction). On HSDC's framing, the disagreement with KSC is not
  about whether to provide guidance — both sides say yes — but about
  *what kinds of guidance* count and what *outcomes* matter.
- **Internal concession.** HSDC concede that they do not yet know the
  optimal balance between scaffolded inquiry and direct instructional
  guidance (p. 106). This is symmetric to KSC's expertise-reversal
  concession: both sides acknowledge their own claim is bounded. The
  honest read of the two papers together is *not* "KSC refuted" or
  "HSDC won," but "the empirical case for either pure pedagogy is weaker
  than partisans of either treat it as, and the productive question is
  conditional on learner, domain, and outcome."
- **Outcome-measure tension.** HSDC argue that "Does it work?" is the wrong
  question (p. 105) because PBL/IL aim at outcomes (epistemic practices,
  collaboration, self-directed learning) that don't appear on standardized
  knowledge tests. This is a real argument — but it cuts both ways. If
  the case for PBL/IL depends on outcomes that are not the outcomes
  currently measured, the empirical strength of the case is partly an
  artifact of *which* studies HSDC are willing to count. The thesis should
  not let either side define the outcome metric unilaterally.
- **Effect-size-vs-design tension in the empirical anchors.** The Geier et
  al. and Lynch et al. studies are quasi-experimental, not RCTs, and the
  effect sizes (0.37–0.44 for Geier; "higher gains" for Lynch) are
  moderate rather than overwhelming. HSDC do not engage selection-bias
  worries common to inquiry-PBL classroom studies (HSDC briefly note
  "self-selection bias in most studies of PBL" on p. 103 in the context
  of the Patel et al. critique). The thesis should weight these as good
  but not definitive evidence — the same epistemic standard the protocol
  applies to KSC.
