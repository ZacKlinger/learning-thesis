## Thesis

A genre shift from Cuban 2024. Where the November 2024 post was Cuban
*predicting* — his historian's voice applying his Cuban-1986 frame to AI —
the March 2025 post is Cuban *curating*. He opens with a single declarative
sentence ("I have gathered comments from teachers who have used AI in daily
lessons and grading student work") and then steps out of the frame. The body
is teacher voices, lightly edited, drawn from press accounts and direct
quotes. Cuban offers no analysis, no concluding paragraph, no prediction.
That structure is the argument: the absorption Cuban 2024 predicted is now
visible, and Cuban thinks the teachers' own descriptions of what they are
doing are worth letting stand without rebuttal.

The selection is illustrative rather than representative. Most of the cited
uses cluster on workload relief (faster feedback on writing, summer lesson
planning, adapting readings for ESL/lower reading levels, drafting parent
emails and recommendation letters). A smaller but visible set cluster on
pedagogical change (students critiquing AI-generated essays on *1984* /
*The Handmaid's Tale* and on *Romeo and Juliet*; precalculus students
prompting ChatGPT to rap about vectors). One teacher (Jen Roberts) reports
that this has been "the best year yet of nearly three decades of teaching"
specifically *because* automated feedback lets her hand out more writing
assignments than she could otherwise grade. The same teacher also admits
she only spot-checks the feedback the AI gives her students — a frank
description of partial substitution of teacher judgment, not just
augmentation. The post documents that ambiguity but does not resolve it.

## Methods (if empirical)

Not empirical; a curated blog post. The teacher quotes appear to be drawn
from press reporting (CalMatters is named in the caption for the Roberts
photo) and from teacher submissions Cuban solicited. Cuban does not name
his sources for every quote, does not describe a sampling frame, and does
not attempt to weight the examples. The Roberts paragraphs read as
near-verbatim lifts from a longer reported piece, but Cuban does not
hyperlink the source. Treat as illustrative anecdote, not evidence about
base rates or representativeness.

## Where this sits in the canon

- The natural pair to Cuban 2024. The two posts read together are a
  three-month longitudinal data point on Cuban's own thinking: in
  November 2024 he predicts that AI will be absorbed into existing
  classroom contours; in March 2025 he documents — by curating, without
  comment — that exact absorption beginning to happen.
- Cross-listed (cited in `claim-evidence/teacher-agency.md`) as primary
  evidence of teacher-led AI adoption clustering on workload relief —

  examples are direct workload-relief uses.
- Cross-listed (cited in `claim-evidence/augmentation-not-efficiency.md`)
  for the Millard "they wanted to beat the computer" example, which is
  one of the few in the post that look like the kind of learning the

- Cross-listed (cited in `claim-evidence/tech-cycles-in-education.md`)

  data point on how the AI wave is moving relative to prior waves Cuban

## Tensions / contradictions

- **vs. Cuban 2024 directly.** In 2024 Cuban concludes that AI advocates
  "fail to understand the complexity of teaching" and predicts AI will
  not transform what teachers do. Cuban 2025 shows teachers genuinely
  benefiting from AI in ways that do not require them to teach
  differently, but also at least one example (Roberts on writing-feedback
  volume) where what *is* changing is the load-bearing mechanism of the
  course — students now get faster feedback on more drafts than the
  teacher could otherwise grade. That is a change in the *adoption
  pathway*, not just a surface-level absorption. Cuban 2024's prediction
  ("AI may become just another item added to the list of previously
  hyped technological innovations") survives in attenuated form: the
  absorption is happening, but it is teacher-led and operates at the
  layer where AI's marginal cost differs from prior waves.
- **Within the post: augmentation vs. partial substitution.** Roberts'
  candid admission that she does not look at the per-student feedback the
  AI generates is exactly the category-error pattern the teacher-agency
  thread is built to resist. The same source is supporting evidence for
  "teacher-led adoption clusters on the four constraints" *and* for "the
  thesis owes a specific account of which AI uses cross the line into
  substituting for the teacher's judgment." Both readings are honest;
  neither dominates.
- **Selection bias.** Cuban chose teachers who use AI productively. The
  post is silent on the teachers who tried and stopped, who refused, who
  were forced to use it by administrators, who had bad experiences with
  AI grading their own kids' work, or who are quietly downgrading
  students whose work *seems* AI-generated. The bottom-up adoption story
  is real but partial.
- **vs. the optimistic thesis directly.** The Ballaret "taking back my
  summer" example is the cleanest match in the current ledger for the
  steer's constraint #3 (system-imposed workload). The Wallace
  "instantly produce simplified versions of readings" example is the
  cleanest match for constraint #2 (one-way nature of fixed content).
  Both teachers report being able to do something the system previously
  forced them to compromise on. Read inside the steer's four-constraint
  frame, this post is a small but specific positive datum.
