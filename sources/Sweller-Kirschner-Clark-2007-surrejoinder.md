## Thesis

The published sur-rejoinder by Sweller, Kirschner & Clark to the two
commentaries on their 2006 paper — Schmidt, Loyens, van Gog & Paas (2007)
and Hmelo-Silver, Duncan & Chinn (2007). The piece does *not* concede ground;
it reasserts and sharpens the original argument. Three moves matter for the
ledger:

(1) **The worked-example effect as the empirical anchor.** SKC argue that
the worked-example effect — the demonstrated superiority of fully-worked
solutions over problem-solving search for novice learners — is the
load-bearing empirical finding that *any* approach claiming to be effective
for novices has to engage. PBL, on their view, deemphasises guidance by
its essential design: "Requiring that a learner discover a problem solution
always reduces guidance compared to presenting the solution." Schmidt et al.
and Hmelo-Silver et al. do not engage the worked-example effect's
implications for PBL, which SKC treat as a fatal omission.

(2) **The "scaffolding" SKC accept is identical to direct instruction.**
SKC explicitly accept that scaffolding is better than no scaffolding — but
argue that "the ultimate scaffold, providing learners with all information
needed including a complete problem solution—either prior to a task or just-
in-time during a task—is better still." On their view, HSDC's case that PBL
is heavily scaffolded does not rescue PBL; it just describes PBL approaching
the limit of direct instruction. The difference between "guided discovery"
and "discovery learning" is real but the trajectory points toward fully
worked examples.

(3) **The "no general teachable problem-solving strategy" claim.** SKC argue
that despite decades of effort, no novel, teachable, general problem-solving
strategy has been isolated. Cognitive architecture supports accumulating
*domain-specific* knowledge in long-term memory, not the acquisition of
general thinking strategies. This cuts at the entire deeper-learning frame
that the ambition-over-complacency thread leans on — the claim that
"critical thinking" or "creative problem-solving" can be taught as
transferable skills.

For the thesis, this is **symmetry**. The KSC ↔ HSDC exchange on the ledger
was previously one-sided (HSDC's response with no SKC reply). Now both
sides of the published exchange are read. The honest read of the full
exchange does not move much from the prior assessment: KSC make a strong
empirical case bounded to novice/intermediate declarative-knowledge
acquisition; HSDC respond that the bound mischaracterises actual scaffolded
inquiry practice and that the outcomes-that-count question is contested;
SKC sur-rejoinder reasserts the worked-example case and challenges the
existence of general teachable strategies. The debate remains genuinely
open conditional on learner, domain, and outcome.

## Key quoted claims

- (On the design of PBL:) "Their disagreement with our contention that PBL
  deemphasizes guidance is surely in conflict with the essential purpose of
  the technique. Surely the raison d'être of PBL is to deemphasize direct
  instructional guidance? ... Requiring that a learner discover a problem
  solution always reduces guidance compared to presenting the solution."
  — p. 116
- (On the worked-example effect:) "The worked-example effect makes quite
  clear the superiority of providing problem solutions over searching for
  them (e.g. Paas & van Gog, 2006)."
  — p. 116
- (On the worked-example logic applied to PBL:) "If problem solving is
  ineffective as a learning device as suggested by the worked-example
  effect, why does it suddenly become effective when used in PBL? Why is
  the worked-example effect relevant to instruction except when that
  instruction is based on PBL? It is contradictory to both accept the
  validity of the worked-example effect and then not accept its
  consequences in the case of PBL."
  — p. 116
- (On scaffolding's limit:) "while scaffolding, like all guidance for
  novices, is better than no scaffolding, the ultimate scaffold, providing
  learners with all information needed including a complete problem
  solution—either prior to a task or just-in-time during a task—is better
  still."
  — p. 117
- (The general-strategies challenge:) "The failure over many decades to
  isolate a single, novel, teachable, general problem-solving or thinking
  strategy has inhibited that pursuit. As a consequence, there is a
  discord, indeed a contradiction, between the aims of IL and the
  processes our cognitive architecture support."
  — p. 117
- (Mayer-citation reinforcement:) "Specifically he noted that 'to solve
  [mathematical] problems requires the acquisition of large amounts of
  domain-specific knowledge.'"
  — p. 117 (SKC quoting Mayer 1987)

## Methods (if empirical)

Not an empirical paper itself. A theoretical response to two published
commentaries, drawing on the same empirical base as KSC 2006 (cognitive
load theory experiments, the worked-example effect literature, the
historical record of attempts to isolate general thinking strategies). No
new experimental data are reported.

## Where this sits in the canon

- Completes the KSC 2006 → HSDC 2007 → SKC 2007 exchange on the ledger.
  All three pieces of the most-cited methodological debate in 21st-century
  educational psychology are now read.
- Sharpens the augmentation-not-efficiency thread's recorded "KSC bound"
  in two directions:
  - The expertise-reversal concession is preserved (SKC do not retract
    p. 80 of KSC 2006).
  - The scope of "minimally guided" is *not* narrowed to pure-discovery
    — SKC explicitly include scaffolded PBL in the category and argue
    that "guided discovery" approaches the limit of direct instruction.
    The HSDC bounding move is not accepted by SKC.
- Sets up the *Knowledge Building* response (Scardamalia & Bereiter
  1994, read this Saturday): if the worked-example effect's logic
  applies to PBL, does it apply to CSILE's intentional-learning
  environments where the student diagnoses their own need and consults
  outside resources? S&B would say no — the unit of analysis is the
  knowledge-building community, not the individual cognitive load — but
  SKC have not engaged the knowledge-building literature directly, and
  the unit-of-analysis disagreement is unresolved on the ledger.

## Tensions / contradictions

- **vs. HSDC 2007.** Direct. SKC reject HSDC's distinction between
  scaffolded PBL/IL and discovery learning, treating the spectrum as
  pointing toward fully-worked examples at the limit. HSDC's
  "indistinguishable from cognitive-load-theory scaffolding" claim is,
  on SKC's reading, the concession that PBL succeeds *only* as it
  approaches direct instruction.
- **vs. the ambition-over-complacency thread's lean on transferable
  thinking skills.** SKC's "no general teachable problem-solving
  strategy" claim is a direct challenge to the deeper-learning frame
  (Zeiser 2014; Hewlett Foundation's six competencies). If SKC are
  right, the ambition thread's positive claim collapses into "more
  domain-specific knowledge acquisition" — which would be the KSC
  recommendation. The thesis should engage this rather than treat
  general-thinking-skills as a settled outcome.
- **vs. Scardamalia & Bereiter 1994.** Indirect but real. CSILE's design
  choice to put intentionality in the *student* (the INTU note)
  contradicts the SKC architecture in which the system / instructor
  carries the diagnostic load. SKC would predict that CSILE-style
  intentional learning is less efficient than worked-example
  instruction for novice declarative-knowledge acquisition; S&B would
  reply that *the outcome that matters* is the student's ability to
  carry out the diagnostic and decision processes, not the declarative
  knowledge SKC measure. The unit-of-analysis and outcome-of-interest
  disagreements run through every page of this debate.
- **Asymmetric reading list.** SKC have not engaged the knowledge-
  building literature or the broader deeper-learning research. The
  augmentation thread's positive case (Scardamalia & Bereiter, Zeiser)
  has not been engaged by the KSC tradition; the KSC critique (worked-
  example effect, cognitive load) has not been engaged by the
  knowledge-building tradition. This is an *unresolved* methodological
  standoff, not a settled debate; the thesis should resist any framing
  that treats either side as having "won."
