## Thesis

A polemical synthesis of cognitive-load theory and a half-century of experimental
work, arguing that minimally guided instructional approaches (discovery,
problem-based, inquiry, experiential, and constructivist teaching) are
inconsistent with what is known about human cognitive architecture — in
particular, the severe capacity limits of working memory when processing novel
information — and that controlled studies "almost uniformly" favour direct
guided instruction for novice and intermediate learners. The authors name
Bruner (1961), Papert (1980), and Steffe & Gale (1995) as exemplars of the
position they reject. They are careful to distinguish *learning a discipline*
from *practicing a discipline*: confusing the epistemology of an expert with the
pedagogy that produces one is, in their reading, the central error of the
position.

## Key quoted claims

- "The past half-century of empirical research on this issue has provided
  overwhelming and unambiguous evidence that minimal guidance during instruction
  is significantly less effective and efficient than guidance specifically
  designed to support the cognitive processing necessary for learning."
  — p. 76
- "The aim of all instruction is to alter long-term memory. If nothing has
  changed in long-term memory, nothing has been learned."
  — p. 77
- "Any instructional theory that ignores the limits of working memory when
  dealing with novel information or ignores the disappearance of those limits
  when dealing with familiar information is unlikely to be effective."
  — p. 77
- "Most learners of all ages know how to construct knowledge when given adequate
  information and there is no evidence that presenting them with partial
  information enhances their ability to construct a representation more than
  giving them full information. Actually, quite the reverse seems most often to
  be true."
  — p. 78
- "The advantage of guidance begins to recede only when learners have
  sufficiently high prior knowledge to provide 'internal' guidance."
  — p. 75 (abstract)
- "Each new set of advocates for unguided approaches seemed either unaware of or
  uninterested in previous evidence that unguided approaches had not been
  validated. This pattern produced discovery learning, which gave way to
  experiential learning, which gave way to problem-based and inquiry learning,
  which now gives way to constructivist instructional techniques."
  — p. 79
- "After a half-century of advocacy associated with instruction using minimal
  guidance, it appears that there is no body of research supporting the
  technique. In so far as there is any evidence from controlled studies, it
  almost uniformly supports direct, strong instructional guidance rather than
  constructivist-based minimal guidance during the instruction of novice to
  intermediate learners."
  — pp. 83–84
- "It is regrettable that current constructivist views have become ideological
  and often epistemologically opposed to the presentation and explanation of
  knowledge."
  — p. 84
- (On the *expertise reversal effect*, quoting their own line of work:) "the
  worked-example effect first disappears and then reverses as the learners'
  expertise increases. Problem solving only becomes relatively effective when
  learners are sufficiently experienced so that studying a worked example is,
  for them, a redundant activity."
  — p. 80

## Methods (if empirical)

Not an empirical paper itself; a theoretical/review essay. The evidence
adduced is (i) cognitive load theory grounded in Atkinson & Shiffrin (1968),
Miller (1956), Sweller (1988); (ii) controlled experimental studies on worked
examples (Sweller & Cooper 1985 and many replications); (iii) reviews
(Mayer 2004; Klahr & Nigam 2004); (iv) meta-analyses of problem-based
learning in medical education (Albanese & Mitchell 1993; Berkson 1993;
Colliver 2000). No new data are reported.

## Tensions / contradictions

- **KSC vs. Papert (1980).** KSC name Papert as exemplifying the position they
  attack. Papert's "Gears of My Childhood" (see `sources/Papert-1980-gears-of-my-childhood.md`)
  argues that the most powerful learning is intrinsically motivated, affectively
  charged, and proceeds through self-discovered assimilation rather than
  external guidance. KSC reply that this confuses descriptive observations about
  learning with prescriptive instructional design. The seriousness of the
  collision: KSC's evidentiary claim is over *novices*; Papert's account is of
  himself as a self-directed learner with extensive prior knowledge from age
  two. The expertise-reversal effect KSC themselves invoke (p. 80) means their
  finding is bounded to novices and does not straightforwardly refute Papert's
  account of his own development. This is a real tension but not the full
  refutation it is sometimes cited as.
- **KSC vs. the published rejoinder (Hmelo-Silver, Duncan & Chinn 2007).** Not
  yet read. The rejoinder is reported to argue (a) that PBL and inquiry as
  practiced are not "minimally guided" — they are heavily scaffolded — and
  (b) that KSC mischaracterise the constructivist literature by lumping
  pedagogically distinct approaches. Until HSDC is in hand, KSC's claims are
  represented here but not endorsed.
- **Internal tension.** KSC concede (p. 80) that the worked-example effect
  reverses for higher-expertise learners. This concession means the "direct
  guidance is superior" claim is bounded to novices. Strong forms of the
  KSC-as-refutation-of-all-discovery-learning argument exceed what the paper
  itself supports.
