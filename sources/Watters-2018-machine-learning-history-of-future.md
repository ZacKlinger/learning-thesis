## Thesis

Watters' two-assertion frame: (1) the role of corporations and philanthropists in
shaping education policy is *not* new — the ed-tech industry has been at this for
over a century, and the marketing rhetoric has *always* been the same: their
products will "augment what teachers can do. Augment, not replace, of course";
(2) the "revolutions" these companies have promised have always failed to
materialise. The body of the piece then historicises AI specifically as the
latest instance of that recurring pattern, with Herbert Simon (1965) and
Marvin Minsky (1970) AI predictions as the contemporary parallels to Edison
(1922) on film. Watters' positive move beyond the cyclical-disappointment
diagnosis is to relocate the load-bearing critique: the danger of the current
wave is not (only) that ed-tech will fail to deliver its promised gains; it is
that the *infrastructure being built around that promise* — proprietary "black
box" decision systems, biased training data, algorithmic prediction sold as
"personalization" — is itself a new and material harm regardless of whether
the optimistic predictions land. The piece pre-dates the November 2022 ChatGPT
moment by four years and the corporate AI-in-education push it set off; its
predictive value for the current AI-in-education wave is therefore directly
testable, which is exactly why it was queued for the tech-cycles thread.

Read against the thesis seeds, Watters 2018 does two things simultaneously.
First, she names the *augmentation-not-replacement* rhetoric as a marketing
trope that has been in the corporate ed-tech playbook for over a century —
i.e., the thesis-seed v1 framing ("augmentation, not efficiency") is itself
on Watters' list of historically-repeating marketing claims, not a fresh
distinction the thesis is inventing. Second, she reroutes the critique from
"will it work?" to "what is being built, and who benefits regardless of
whether it works?" — surveillance, predictive scoring, infrastructural lock-in
into proprietary systems. That second move is *not* the steelman the
thesis-seed v4 thread was originally constructed against; it is a different
class of objection, and it cuts at the optimistic case from an angle the
prior canon entries (KSC, Cuban, Jordan) do not.

## Key quoted claims

- "These companies and their league of marketers and advocates have long argued
  that their products will augment what teachers can do. Augment, not replace,
  of course. Their products will make teachers' work easier, faster, companies
  have always promised."
  — Watters 2018, ¶4
- "as long as these companies and their evangelists have been pitching their
  products to schools, they have promised a 'revolution.' ... So far at least
  these predictions have always been wrong."
  — Watters 2018, ¶5
- "Educational films were going to change everything. Teaching machines were
  going to change everything. Educational television was going to change
  everything. Virtual reality was going to change everything. The Internet was
  going to change everything. The Macintosh computer was going to change
  everything. The iPad was going to change everything. Khan Academy was going
  to change everything. MOOCs were going to change everything. And on and on
  and on."
  — Watters 2018, ¶7
- "The transformational, revolutionary potential of these technologies has
  always been vastly, vastly overhyped. And it isn't simply, as some education
  reformers like to tell it, that it's because educators or parents are
  resistant to change. It's surely in part because the claims that marketers
  make are often just simply untrue."
  — Watters 2018, ¶10
- "This promised 'ed-tech revolution' hasn't occurred either in part because
  the predictions that technologists make are so often divorced from the
  realities of institutional and individual practices, from the cultures,
  systems, beliefs, and values of schools and their communities."
  — Watters 2018, ¶11
- "There is arguably no better example of this than the predictions made about
  artificial intelligence."
  — Watters 2018, ¶12
- "in 1965, for example, Herbert Simon said that 'machines will be capable,
  within twenty years, of doing any work a man can do.' In 1970, Marvin Minsky
  said that 'in from three to eight years, we will have a machine with the
  general intelligence of an average human being.' Fifty, sixty years later,
  we still don't."
  — Watters 2018, ¶13
- "anytime you hear someone say 'personalization' or 'AI' or 'algorithmic,' I
  urge you to replace that phrase with 'prediction.'"
  — Watters 2018, ¶21
- "all algorithms are biased. I know we are being told that these algorithms
  are better, smarter, faster, more accurate but they are, as a recent RSA
  video put it, 'an opinion embedded in math.'"
  — Watters 2018, ¶21
- "this may well be a revolution, and it's not one that its advocates
  necessarily predicted or is it, I'd wager one any of us want to be part of."
  — Watters 2018, closing line

## Methods (if empirical)

Not empirical. Prepared remarks for a 15-minute conference panel slot,
expanded for the blog. The piece works in the genre of polemic-with-historical-
examples — Watters' standard form. Her supporting evidence is historical
(the century-long pattern of corporate ed-tech marketing) and contemporary
case-study (Knewton; IBM Watson). The strongest argument is by analogy and
demonstration: she shows that "AI in education" is the latest move in a
recurring corporate pitch with the same structure (revolution promised,
revolution not delivered, the next vehicle for the same promise rolls out).
The black-box / bias / political-economy section invokes Pasquale 2015
(*The Black Box Society*) and Noble 2018 (*The Algorithms of Oppression*)
as the theoretical backing — both worth pursuing if the thread wants to
deepen the critical-edtech leg.

## Where this sits in the canon

- Cross-listed (`claim-evidence/tech-cycles-in-education.md`) as the
  contemporary critical-edtech anchor for the cyclical-hype framing, paired
  with the Cuban blog corpus. Watters and Cuban agree on the pattern but
  arrive at it differently — Cuban from inside the historian's frame of
  classroom practice ("teachers absorb tech without changing how they
  teach"); Watters from outside the institution, focused on the corporate-
  and-philanthropic apparatus that sells the technology in the first place.
- Cross-listed (`claim-evidence/augmentation-not-efficiency.md`) because the
  "augment what teachers can do. Augment, not replace, of course" line is a
  direct historicisation of the thesis-seed v1 framing. The thread now owes
  an account of what makes the thesis's use of "augmentation" different from
  the century of marketing rhetoric Watters identifies — or an admission
  that the rhetorical move is older than the thesis assumed.
- The 2018 date matters operationally for the tech-cycles thread. Watters
  predates the November 2022 ChatGPT moment by four years. Her "AI in
  education" examples in 2018 are Knewton ("mind reading robot tutor in the
  sky") and IBM Watson. The contemporary AI-in-education wave (LLM-based
  chatbots) post-dates this essay; the predictive value of her 2018 framing
  for the current wave is therefore one of the cleanest tests available of
  whether the cyclical-hype frame survives contact with generative AI
  specifically. Read against Cuban 2025 (curated teacher voices, March
  2025), Watters' 2018 forecast is partially confirmed (the rhetoric of
  "personalization" is intact) and partially complicated (the bottom-up
  teacher-led adoption Cuban documents is structurally different from the
  top-down adoption Watters spends most of her piece criticising).

## Tensions / contradictions

- **vs. the thesis's "augmentation, not efficiency" framing.** Watters'
  identification of "augment, not replace" as a century-old corporate
  marketing trope is a real complication for the thesis's framing. The
  thesis is not arguing what Pearson and IBM have been arguing — but the
  shared vocabulary is a problem the thesis owes a response to. Either the
  thesis's "augmentation" is a different *thing* from the marketing claim
  (and the thesis owes a definition that distinguishes them), or the
  shared vocabulary should be retired in favour of a less freighted term.
  This complication directly serves the steer-rejected synthesis the
  2026-05-27 steer warned against — i.e., "AI builds the floor first, then
  enables ambition later" reads in Watters' frame as exactly the
  augment-not-replace pitch she is calling out. The steer's choice to
  reject that synthesis is *strengthened* by Watters' historicisation.
- **vs. the steer's tech-cycles-as-setup framing.** The steer reframed
  tech-cycles from "every prior wave failed, so this one must prove itself"
  (foil) to "the medium has been changing all along, AI is the next change"
  (setup). Watters 2018 sits inside the foil framing, not the setup
  framing. Her position is: the medium-change rhetoric is itself the
  marketing claim, and the actual change in each prior wave has been to
  the political-economic infrastructure of schooling (corporate capture,
  data extraction, lock-in), not to learning. The steer-reframed thread
  can take Watters' historical evidence without taking her conclusion,
  but it has to do that work explicitly. The current state of the thread
  does not yet do that work; the Watters entry below makes the gap visible.
- **vs. Cuban 2024 / Cuban 2025.** Watters and Cuban arrive at largely the
  same diagnosis from different sides — Cuban from inside the classroom
  practice frame, Watters from outside the institution looking at the
  industry. Where they differ is in the affordance: Cuban 2025 documents
  teachers genuinely benefiting from AI on workload and content-adaptation
  uses; Watters 2018 does not predict that mode at all — her teachers are
  passive recipients of top-down corporate "solutions," not bottom-up
  adopters of a free or near-free general-purpose chatbot. The 2018-to-
  2025 gap is the missing data: the actual emergence of free, high-quality,
  bottom-up-adopted general-purpose AI tools (ChatGPT et al.) is not a
  category Watters' 2018 frame anticipated.
- **vs. Papert 1980 / Illich 1971 Ch 6.** The visionary supporting case
  in the augmentation thread (Papert's gears; Illich's peer-matching
  networks) describes learner-controlled, non-commercial uses of
  technology. Watters' critique is specifically aimed at *corporate* ed-
  tech — the commercial pipeline running from Silicon Valley into the
  classroom. The thesis can in principle accept Watters' critique of the
  corporate pipeline while claiming the Papert/Illich-Ch-6 vision as its
  positive frame — but only if it specifies the *deployment* it has in
  mind. Watters' line ("the predictions that technologists make are so
  often divorced from the realities of institutional and individual
  practices") names exactly the failure mode Papert's Logo evaluation
  history documents in detail; this is the same gap from a different
  angle, and it strengthens rather than weakens the case for an
  operational definition of "previously impossible learning."
