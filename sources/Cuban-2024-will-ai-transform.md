## Thesis

Cuban's compact contemporary statement of his classic position, applied to AI.
Adapted from a five-member Google panel he sat on as the lone historian. The
argument has three moves: (1) every prior classroom technology of the last
century — radio, 16mm film, overhead projectors, instructional TV,
videocassettes, 1:1 laptops, interactive whiteboards — was introduced with
"revolutionary" and "transformational" rhetoric; (2) none of them produced
evidence of fundamental change in how teachers teach, student engagement, or
test scores; (3) the reason this happens is not that the technologies are
deficient but that AI advocates "fail to understand the complexity of
teaching," in particular the emotional bonds between teacher and student.
Cuban's specific prediction: AI will be adapted by teachers "to fit the
contours of their classrooms" — added to "the list of previously hyped
technological innovations that evoked initial gasps of delight and slowly
became part of many teachers' repertoires."

This is a primary, contemporary, AI-specific statement of the canonical
Cuban argument, written at the height of the current hype cycle by the
historian whose 1986 book defined the genre. It substitutes — partially,
not fully — for *Teachers and Machines*, which remains paywalled.

## Key quoted claims

- "Over the past century, every technology introduced to improve teaching
  and learning has been hyped as 'revolutionary' and 'transformational.'"
  — ¶4
- "Yet there is little evidence that classroom use of these previous
  technologies forced classroom teachers to rethink, much less reshape,
  their instruction. Nor have I found convincing evidence that these
  technologies altered fundamentally how teachers teach, increased student
  engagement, or raised test scores."
  — ¶8
- "So I have concluded that those pushing AI use in classrooms fail to
  understand the complexity of teaching."
  — ¶9
- "Teaching, then, is not a mechanical act of connecting dots. Teaching is
  a complex act that requires knowledge of subject matter, managerial
  skills, and emotional labor. Often, it is improvisational. Most
  important, however, is that teaching requires gaining students' trust.
  It is both an art and a science that takes years to master."
  — ¶13
- "While I believe AI will not force practitioners to rethink how they
  teach, nonetheless, as so many teachers have done in the past, they will
  adapt AI to fit the contours of their classrooms. And in doing so, AI may
  become just another item added to the list of previously hyped
  technological innovations that evoked initial gasps of delight and slowly
  became part of many teachers' repertoires."
  — ¶15
- (Quoting the Stanford GSE Dean to set the foil:) "[This Technology] is a
  game-changer for education — it offers the prospect of universal access
  to high-quality learning experiences, and it creates fundamentally new
  ways of teaching."
  — ¶6 (Cuban quoting; not Cuban's claim)
- "schools are just as vulnerable to technological fads as are women's
  fashions, deodorants, and automobile styling." — footnote (Cuban's
  characteristically dry summary of the dynamic)

## Methods (if empirical)

Not empirical; opinion essay built on Cuban's lifetime of historical work in
*Teachers and Machines* (1986), *Oversold and Underused* (2001), and
*Tinkering Toward Utopia* (with Tyack, 1995). The historical premise is
Cuban's own scholarship; the AI-specific claims are predictions. No data
are reported in this post itself.

## Where this sits in the canon

- A *contemporary, AI-aware* statement of the Cuban position. Useful as a
  primary-source substitute for *Teachers and Machines* (1986) while that
  book remains paywalled.
- Per the 2026-05-27 steer, tech-cycles is **setup**, not foil — "the
  medium of learning has been changing throughout history; AI is the
  latest change." Cuban's argument fits the setup framing only if we read
  past his own conclusion: Cuban himself thinks the medium *does not*
  fundamentally change what teachers do. He is making the opposite case to
  "the medium evolves toward better learning." The thesis should not
  assimilate him into its own frame.
- Cross-listed (cited in `claim-evidence/tech-cycles-in-education.md`) as
  the cleanest concise statement of the wave-by-wave hype pattern.
- Cross-listed (cited in `claim-evidence/teacher-agency.md`) for the
  "teaching is complex / emotional bonds matter" claim, which lands on
  the same point Dewey 1897 makes from a different angle — and which
  *agrees* with the steer's reframing of the teacher-agency thread (AI is
  not a route around teacher relationships; it is a tool teachers use).

## Tensions / contradictions

- **vs. the optimistic thesis directly.** Cuban does not think AI will
  transform teaching. The thesis says AI removes four constraints (cost of
  expert attention; one-way fixed content; system-imposed workload;
  gap to expert practice). Cuban's reply is: that is exactly the rhetoric
  every prior wave used, and the classroom adoption pathway absorbed all
  of them. The thesis owes Cuban a specific account of *what is
  structurally different this time* in the adoption pathway, not in the
  capability. (Per the steer: this is the question internal to the
  defense, not a burden the thesis has to clear before mounting one.)
- **vs. Watters.** Cuban (2024) and Watters (in her hackeducation.com
  corpus, sources-raw/) are usually grouped together, but their critiques
  differ. Cuban is institutional/sociological (the classroom absorbs
  technology); Watters is political/economic (edtech is mostly a market
  story dressed as a pedagogy story). The next-Saturday reading should
  pair them rather than treat them as substitutes.
- **vs. Cuban himself, 2025.** Cuban-2025-some-teachers-use-of-ai.html in
  sources-raw/ moderates this position — he reports specific teachers
  finding genuine value in AI tools. Worth reading next to update the
  Cuban position from "AI will be absorbed" to Cuban's own evolving view.
- **Genre note.** This is a blog post, not peer-reviewed scholarship.
  Citing it with the same evidentiary weight as Cuban 1986 would be a
  category error. The thesis can use it as a contemporary primary-source
  statement of Cuban's *position*; the historical claim it rests on
  (every prior wave failed to transform teaching) still requires *Teachers
  and Machines* to be read in full.
