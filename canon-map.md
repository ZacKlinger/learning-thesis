## Empirical (cognitive science, ed psych, learning sciences)

### [Kirschner, Sweller & Clark 2006] Why Minimal Guidance During Instruction Does Not Work
- Layer: empirical
- Why monumental: The canonical critique of constructivist / discovery / problem-based / inquiry teaching. Anchors the cognitive-load-theory counter-argument that any "ambitious learning" thesis must steelman.
- Read status: queued
- Source note: —
- Relates to: Papert (1980); Bransford et al. (2000); Sweller (1988); Hmelo-Silver, Duncan & Chinn (2007) — the published rejoinder.

### [Bransford, Brown & Cocking, eds. 2000] How People Learn: Brain, Mind, Experience, and School (NRC)
- Layer: empirical
- Why monumental: The synthesis document for the learning sciences; the field's shared baseline on prior knowledge, transfer, expert/novice differences, metacognition.
- Read status: queued
- Source note: —
- Relates to: Chi/Feltovich/Glaser (1981); Bruner (1960); Hattie (2009).

### [Roediger & Karpicke 2006] Test-Enhanced Learning: Taking Memory Tests Improves Long-Term Retention
- Layer: empirical
- Why monumental: Anchors the retrieval-practice literature, one of the most robust empirical effects in learning research.
- Read status: queued
- Source note: —
- Relates to: Dunlosky et al. (2013).

### [Dunlosky, Rawson, Marsh, Nathan & Willingham 2013] Improving Students' Learning With Effective Learning Techniques
- Layer: empirical
- Why monumental: Authoritative ranking of study techniques by evidentiary support. Useful as a forcing function: which "ambitious" pedagogies clear the bar?
- Read status: queued
- Source note: —
- Relates to: Roediger & Karpicke (2006).

### [Bloom 1984] The 2 Sigma Problem: The Search for Methods of Group Instruction as Effective as One-to-One Tutoring
- Layer: empirical
- Why monumental: The most-cited claim about what individualized instruction can achieve — and the empirical hook AI-tutoring proponents lean on. Effect size and replication status both need scrutiny.
- Read status: queued
- Source note: —
- Relates to: Khan (2012); intelligent tutoring literature.

### [Sweller 1988] Cognitive Load During Problem Solving: Effects on Learning
- Layer: empirical
- Why monumental: The founding paper of cognitive load theory, the mechanism Kirschner/Sweller/Clark (2006) build on.
- Read status: queued
- Source note: —
- Relates to: Kirschner, Sweller & Clark (2006).

### [Chi, Feltovich & Glaser 1981] Categorization and Representation of Physics Problems by Experts and Novices
- Layer: empirical
- Why monumental: The classic expert/novice study; the empirical ground for "deep features vs. surface features" in problem representation. Sets evidentiary anchor for what mastery actually consists of.
- Read status: queued
- Source note: —

### [Anderson, Reder & Simon 1996] Situated Learning and Education
- Layer: empirical
- Why monumental: The cognitive-science rebuttal to strong situated-cognition / situated-learning claims. Important steelman against romantic "authentic learning" framings.
- Read status: queued
- Source note: —
- Relates to: Lave & Wenger (1991); Greeno et al. — the other side of that debate.

### [Willingham 2009] Why Don't Students Like School?
- Layer: empirical (cognitive psych applied to schooling)
- Why monumental: Most accessible bridge from cognitive science to classroom practice. Strong on the role of background knowledge for thinking — a sober counter to "skills, not facts" framings.
- Read status: queued
- Source note: —

### [Hattie 2009] Visible Learning
- Layer: empirical (meta-meta-analysis)
- Why monumental: The most-cited synthesis of education effect sizes. Also one of the most-criticized; the methodological critiques are part of why this entry matters.
- Read status: queued
- Source note: —
- Relates to: Bergeron & Rivard (2017) critique; Simpson (2017) critique.

### [Deci, Koestner & Ryan 1999] A Meta-Analytic Review of Experiments Examining the Effects of Extrinsic Rewards on Intrinsic Motivation
- Layer: empirical
- Why monumental: Anchor for the motivation literature; the empirical claim that extrinsic incentives can undermine intrinsic engagement — central to any argument about what schooling does to curiosity.
- Read status: queued
- Source note: —

### [Hmelo-Silver, Duncan & Chinn 2007] Scaffolding and Achievement in Problem-Based and Inquiry Learning
- Layer: empirical
- Why monumental: The published response to Kirschner/Sweller/Clark (2006). Must be read together with that paper; reading one without the other gives a distorted picture.
- Read status: queued
- Source note: —
- Relates to: Kirschner, Sweller & Clark (2006).

## Visionary / philosophical

### [Dewey 1897] My Pedagogic Creed
- Layer: visionary
- Why monumental: The shortest distillation of Dewey's view of school as social institution and the teacher as a member of a community. Foundational for any "teacher agency" thread.
- Read status: queued
- Source note: —
- Relates to: Dewey (1916); Dewey (1938).

### [Dewey 1916] Democracy and Education
- Layer: visionary
- Why monumental: Dewey's mature theory connecting the form of school to the form of democratic life. The deepest text behind contemporary "deeper learning" rhetoric.
- Read status: queued
- Source note: —

### [Dewey 1938] Experience and Education
- Layer: visionary
- Why monumental: Dewey's late correction of "progressive education" excess — he warned against the romantic misreading of his own work. A critical guardrail against naive constructivism.
- Read status: queued
- Source note: —

### [Whitehead 1929] The Aims of Education and Other Essays
- Layer: visionary
- Why monumental: The "inert ideas" critique and the rhythm of education (romance / precision / generalization). Central to any "ambition over complacency" argument.
- Read status: queued
- Source note: —

### [Bruner 1960] The Process of Education
- Layer: visionary (with empirical orientation)
- Why monumental: The Woods Hole report; introduced the "spiral curriculum" and the claim that any subject can be taught in some honest form at any age. Anchors structure-of-the-disciplines thinking.
- Read status: queued
- Source note: —

### [Bruner 1966] Toward a Theory of Instruction
- Layer: visionary / empirical
- Why monumental: Bruner's articulation of instructional theory — readiness, structure, sequence, reinforcement. Bridge between psychology and pedagogy.
- Read status: queued
- Source note: —

### [Vygotsky 1978] Mind in Society
- Layer: visionary / empirical
- Why monumental: The Zone of Proximal Development and the social mediation of higher mental functions. Most-cited theoretical frame in the learning sciences after Piaget.
- Read status: queued
- Source note: —

### [Papert 1980] Mindstorms: Children, Computers, and Powerful Ideas
- Layer: visionary
- Why monumental: The most ambitious argument that computers enable kinds of learning previously impossible — the original positive vision for tech in education that Zack's thesis stands in lineage with. Also the canonical foil for the cognitive-load critique.
- Read status: queued
- Source note: —
- Relates to: Kirschner, Sweller & Clark (2006); Papert (1993).

### [Papert 1993] The Children's Machine: Rethinking School in the Age of the Computer
- Layer: visionary
- Why monumental: Papert's diagnosis of why schools absorb technology without changing — the "School" (capital S) as immune system. Direct ancestor of the "augmentation, not efficiency" thread.
- Read status: queued
- Source note: —

### [Illich 1971] Deschooling Society
- Layer: visionary
- Why monumental: The strongest case that institutional schooling itself is the problem — conflates teaching with learning, credentialing with competence. Steelman threat to any "improve school with AI" framing.
- Read status: queued
- Source note: —

### [Freire 1970] Pedagogy of the Oppressed
- Layer: visionary
- Why monumental: "Banking concept" of education vs. dialogical / problem-posing. Most-cited critique of the teacher-as-transmitter model worldwide.
- Read status: queued
- Source note: —

### [Holt 1964/1967] How Children Fail / How Children Learn
- Layer: visionary / practitioner hybrid
- Why monumental: Phenomenological observation of children in classrooms; the original argument that what schools measure is largely strategy for avoiding intellectual risk.
- Read status: queued
- Source note: —

## Contemporary practitioners

### [Sizer 1984] Horace's Compromise: The Dilemma of the American High School
- Layer: practitioner
- Why monumental: The canonical observation that the American high school is held together by a tacit compromise — teachers, students, and parents agree to demand less in exchange for peace. The structural diagnosis Zack's "ambition over complacency" thread restates.
- Read status: queued
- Source note: —

### [Meier 1995] The Power of Their Ideas: Lessons for America from a Small School in Harlem
- Layer: practitioner
- Why monumental: Central Park East — proof of existence that a public school can hold ambitious habits-of-mind as its core, at scale, in a high-poverty setting. Specific, replicable, documented.
- Read status: queued
- Source note: —

### [Mehta & Fine 2019] In Search of Deeper Learning: The Quest to Remake the American High School
- Layer: practitioner / empirical hybrid
- Why monumental: Multi-year ethnography of high schools attempting deeper learning. Honest about how rare it is and where it appears (often in electives and clubs, not core classes). Useful corrective to school-design hype.
- Read status: queued
- Source note: —

### [Greenberg 1995] Free at Last: The Sudbury Valley School
- Layer: practitioner
- Why monumental: 50+ year proof of existence of a radically un-curricularized school. Either an existence proof for Illich or an edge case that doesn't generalize — both readings need engagement.
- Read status: queued
- Source note: —

### [Khan 2012] The One World Schoolhouse: Education Reimagined
- Layer: practitioner
- Why monumental: The most-distributed contemporary articulation of tech-enabled mastery learning. Direct test case for "augmentation vs. efficiency" — which is it actually doing?
- Read status: queued
- Source note: —

### [Lemov 2010] Teach Like a Champion
- Layer: practitioner
- Why monumental: The most influential articulation of the structured-teaching tradition; the practitioner counter to "teacher autonomy at all costs." Required steelman for the teacher-agency thread.
- Read status: queued
- Source note: —

### [Wagner 2008] The Global Achievement Gap
- Layer: practitioner / policy
- Why monumental: Articulates the "seven survival skills" frame that became the deeper-learning / 21st-century-skills agenda. Both influence and target of criticism.
- Read status: queued
- Source note: —

### [Ravitch 2010] The Death and Life of the Great American School System
- Layer: practitioner / policy
- Why monumental: A reformer's recantation — Ravitch reversing her earlier support for accountability/choice. Highest-credibility insider critique of the policy era that birthed scripted curricula.
- Read status: queued
- Source note: —

### [High Tech High collected writings — Rosenstock, Riordan, et al.]
- Layer: practitioner
- Why monumental: HTH is the most documented contemporary project-based-learning network. The unpublished sermons (e.g., Rosenstock essays, Riordan's pieces in *Unboxed*) are the primary material.
- Read status: queued (need to identify a stable canonical text)
- Source note: —

### [Acton Academy / Synthesis — primary writings, Jeff Sandefer; Josh Dahn / Chrisman Frank]
- Layer: practitioner
- Why monumental: Two contemporary networks claiming a fundamentally different model — Acton's hero's-journey + Socratic discussions, Synthesis's complex collaborative games. Primary writings, not the press coverage, are the canon.
- Read status: queued (need to identify stable primary texts)
- Source note: —

### [Hirsch 1987] Cultural Literacy: What Every American Needs to Know
- Layer: practitioner / philosophical
- Why monumental: The strongest articulation of the knowledge-rich-curriculum tradition. A non-trivial counter to any framing that valorizes "skills, not content," and the intellectual root of the Core Knowledge sequence.
- Read status: queued
- Source note: —

### [Tough 2012] How Children Succeed: Grit, Curiosity, and the Hidden Power of Character
- Layer: practitioner / popular synthesis
- Why monumental: Popular bridge from non-cognitive-skills research (Heckman, Duckworth) to practitioner discourse. Useful entry point — and useful site to audit, given subsequent critiques of grit research.
- Read status: queued
- Source note: —

## Tech cycles in education (cross-cutting)

A fourth thematic layer added 2026-05-12, supporting thesis-seed #4. Entries here are mostly historical or media-ecological — they document the pattern that every prior wave of educational technology followed. They are the load-bearing steelman against "this time is different" claims, including ones the thesis seeds make implicitly. Several entries cross-list (Tyack & Cuban 1995 is already in the visionary/historical canon; Cuban appears in practitioner-historian mode here).

### [Cuban 1986] Teachers and Machines: The Classroom Use of Technology Since 1920
- Layer: historical / empirical (practitioner-historian)
- Why monumental: The foundational historical text on the hype-adoption-disappointment cycle. Documents cinema, radio, television, and computers — each introduced with comparable optimistic rhetoric, each largely absorbed or rejected. The base rate any optimistic AI-in-education thesis has to argue *against*.
- Read status: queued
- Source note: —
- Relates to: Cuban (2001); Tyack & Cuban (1995); Watters (2021).

### [Cuban 2001] Oversold and Underused: Computers in the Classroom
- Layer: historical / empirical
- Why monumental: The post-PC-era follow-up to *Teachers and Machines*. Documents the gap between technology access and instructional change: high investment, real availability, modest impact on what teachers actually do.
- Read status: queued
- Source note: —

### [Watters 2021] Teaching Machines: The History of Personalized Learning
- Layer: historical
- Why monumental: The most recent canonical history of "personalized learning" — tracing the lineage from Pressey's testing machine (1920s) through Skinner's teaching machines (1950s–60s) through the computer-assisted instruction movement to today's adaptive-learning platforms. The pattern is the point.
- Read status: queued
- Source note: —

### [Reiser 2001] A History of Instructional Design and Technology: Part I: A History of Instructional Media
- Layer: historical (field survey)
- Why monumental: The standard field-survey paper covering the historical waves. Useful as a map; lighter on critique than Cuban or Watters.
- Read status: queued
- Source note: —

### [Saettler 1990] The Evolution of American Educational Technology
- Layer: historical (reference)
- Why monumental: Dense, comprehensive reference history through the late 20th century. Useful for primary-source citation chains, less for synthesis.
- Read status: queued
- Source note: —

### [Postman 1992] Technopoly: The Surrender of Culture to Technology
- Layer: visionary / media-ecology critique
- Why monumental: The strongest articulation of the media-ecology argument that each new dominant medium reorganizes thought, often invisibly and at cost. Direct challenge to "augmentation" framings that treat tools as neutral.
- Read status: queued
- Source note: —
- Relates to: Postman (1985) *Amusing Ourselves to Death*; Selwyn (2016).

### [Skinner 1958] Teaching Machines
- Layer: visionary / primary document
- Why monumental: The original empirical-and-philosophical case for programmed instruction by machine. A primary document of one of the past waves. Read against contemporary AI-tutoring claims, the structural similarity is the point.
- Read status: queued
- Source note: —

### [Edison 1922] Public statements on cinema as educational medium
- Layer: primary document / hype anchor
- Why monumental: Edison's "I believe that the motion picture is destined to revolutionize our educational system and that in a few years it will supplant largely, if not entirely, the use of textbooks" is the most-quoted historical example of the hype-then-disappointment pattern. Need the actual sourced quote, not the floating-around-the-internet version.
- Read status: queued
- Source note: —

### [Pappano 2012] "The Year of the MOOC"
- Layer: primary document / contemporary hype
- Why monumental: The *New York Times* article that crystallized MOOC hype. Read paired with the academic literature on actual MOOC completion rates and the platform pivots that followed.
- Read status: queued
- Source note: —

### [Selwyn 2016] Is Technology Good for Education?
- Layer: critical / contemporary
- Why monumental: The most prominent contemporary critical-edtech scholar's compact statement of the case. Useful counter-voice in the present.
- Read status: queued
- Source note: —

### [Christensen, Horn & Johnson 2008] Disrupting Class
- Layer: practitioner / theoretical
- Why monumental: The disruption-theory case applied to schooling — the framework many AI-in-education optimists implicitly use. Worth reading because the theory has aged unevenly; its predictions about K-12 are mostly testable now and many have not borne out.
- Read status: queued
- Source note: —

(Cross-listed: Tyack & Cuban (1995) *Tinkering Toward Utopia* — already in the visionary section — is core for this thread too.)
