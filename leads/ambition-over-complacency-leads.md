# Leads — ambition over complacency

Unverified material relevant to the ambition/complacency thread. Nothing here has been read by the agent. Promotion to `claim-evidence/` requires a verbatim quote + URL + access date.

## Primary sources to read

- **Sizer, Theodore R. (1984). *Horace's Compromise: The Dilemma of the American High School*.** The canonical articulation of the compromise that holds the American high school together. Almost any version of the "ambition" thread restates Sizer in some form, so reading the original matters.
- **Tyack, David & Cuban, Larry (1995). *Tinkering Toward Utopia: A Century of Public School Reform*.** Necessary corrective to overconfident "schools are frozen in 1900" narratives. Their account is more interesting: schools change at the edges and have a "grammar of schooling" that absorbs reform.
- **Cuban, Larry (1993). *How Teachers Taught: Constancy and Change in American Classrooms, 1890–1990*.** The empirical detail behind the "grammar of schooling" claim.
- **Hirsch, E.D. (1987). *Cultural Literacy: What Every American Needs to Know*.** The strongest articulation of the knowledge-rich-curriculum tradition and a real counter to "ambition over content."
- **Whitehead, Alfred North (1929). *The Aims of Education and Other Essays*.** The "inert ideas" passage and the rhythm of romance / precision / generalization. Often paraphrased; need the source.
- **Dewey, John (1938). *Experience and Education*.** Dewey's own warning against the romantic misreading of his progressive-education argument. The most important primary text for guarding against complacency-via-progressivism.
- **Mehta, Jal & Fine, Sarah (2019). *In Search of Deeper Learning: The Quest to Remake the American High School*.** Multi-year ethnography. Their finding — that deeper learning is rarer than advertised, often happens in electives and clubs — is the kind of empirically grounded complication this thread needs.
- **Meier, Deborah (1995). *The Power of Their Ideas: Lessons for America from a Small School in Harlem*.** Proof of existence for ambitious schooling at scale in a high-poverty setting.

## Empirical / policy work to locate

- The Committee of Ten report (1893), the Cardinal Principles of Secondary Education (1918), the Conant report (1959). The actual founding documents of the American high school's shape. Most "schools were designed for the industrial era" claims are at odds with what these documents say.
- Labaree, David. *Someone Has to Fail* (2010) — historical sociology of why American schools have the goals they have.
- Deeper Learning Network evaluations (American Institutes for Research, ~2014). What did the multi-year study of deeper-learning schools actually find?
- High Tech High longitudinal outcomes (postsecondary, civic, employment). Their own reporting + independent evaluations.

## Practitioner / commentary leads

- Ted Sizer's Coalition of Essential Schools — its rise and decline as a movement.
- "Common Principles" of CES — short, ambitious, and now mostly absorbed into the broader deeper-learning vocabulary.
- New York City's small-schools movement (Bloomberg-era) — the largest natural experiment on whether ambitious small schools scale.

## Tensions to chase

- The historical claim that schools were "designed for factories" is mostly mythology. The thread is stronger if it engages the actual, more interesting history: schools were designed to do many things at once (sort, socialize, credential, educate), and the compromise is the equilibrium of those goals.
- "Ambition" in the absence of a content theory is empty. Hirsch and the Core Knowledge tradition are the most coherent insistence on this point. The thread should not be allowed to wave that away.
- The strongest empirical evidence that *some* schools achieve ambitious learning at scale comes from specific networks (HTH, EL Education, Big Picture Learning) with specific designs. The lesson may be design-specific rather than a general "tech enables ambition" claim.
