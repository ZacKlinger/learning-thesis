# Leads — tech cycles in education

Unverified material relevant to the tech-cycles thread. Nothing here has been read by the agent. Promotion to `claim-evidence/` requires a verbatim quote + URL + access date.

## Primary sources to read

- **Cuban, Larry (1986). *Teachers and Machines: The Classroom Use of Technology Since 1920*.** Foundational. The book most subsequent edtech history defines itself in conversation with.
- **Cuban, Larry (2001). *Oversold and Underused: Computers in the Classroom*.** Post-PC follow-up.
- **Cuban, Larry (2018). *The Flight of a Butterfly or the Path of a Bullet?: Knowing When to Change Course in High-Tech Reform Efforts*.** Even more recent; specifically on tech-reform timing.
- **Watters, Audrey (2021). *Teaching Machines: The History of Personalized Learning*.** The recent canonical history of "personalized learning" claims.
- **Postman, Neil (1985). *Amusing Ourselves to Death*.** Media-ecology critique of television as a learning medium. The lineage that gets applied to every subsequent screen.
- **Postman, Neil (1992). *Technopoly*.** Broader critique.
- **Selwyn, Neil (2016). *Is Technology Good for Education?*.** Contemporary critical-edtech scholar; compact volume.
- **Reiser, Robert A. (2001). "A History of Instructional Design and Technology: Part I: A History of Instructional Media."** *Educational Technology Research and Development* 49(1). ## Primary documents from each wave (the hype anchors)

- **Edison, Thomas A. (1922).** Public statement that film would revolutionize / supplant the textbook. Need a sourced citation — the floating internet versions vary. Likely traceable to a 1922 interview in *The New York Dramatic Mirror* or similar; verify before quoting.
- **Skinner, B.F. (1954). "The Science of Learning and the Art of Teaching."** *Harvard Educational Review*, 24, 86–97. The opening shot of the teaching-machine wave.
- **Skinner, B.F. (1958). "Teaching Machines."** *Science*, 128(3330), 969–977.
- **Pressey, S.L. (1926). "A Simple Apparatus Which Gives Tests and Scores — And Teaches."** *School & Society*, 23. The original teaching-machine paper, pre-Skinner.
- **Suppes, Patrick (1966). "The Uses of Computers in Education."** *Scientific American*, 215(3). The CAI optimistic statement.
- **Papert, Seymour (1980). *Mindstorms*.** Cross-listed — sits in the visionary canon but is also a primary document of the home-computer-era wave.
- **Pappano, Laura (2012). "The Year of the MOOC."** *The New York Times*, Nov 2, 2012. Hype crystallization.
- **Khan, Salman (2012). *The One World Schoolhouse*.** Cross-listed in practitioner canon. Also a primary document of the video-content / mastery-learning wave.

## Tensions to chase

- **The Cuban argument can be misread two ways.** First, as "tech never works in education" — Cuban is more careful than this; he documents specific reasons adoption fails. Second, as a *prediction* — Cuban describes a historical pattern, not a law of nature. The thread should engage both errors.
- **What is "the grammar of schooling"?** Tyack & Cuban's term. Important to define operationally before invoking it. Otherwise the argument is "schools resist change because schools resist change."
- **Generative AI's adoption pathway may be genuinely novel** in that it is reaching students directly, outside the institution. If so, the "schools absorb tech" record may not constrain it. But also: most studies of out-of-school learning effects on academic outcomes are weak.
- **Counter-examples matter.** The textbook, the blackboard, the calculator, the standardized test booklet — these *are* technologies that reshaped schooling. What made them succeed where film, radio, and TV failed?
- **The personalized-learning lineage is the most direct test case for AI tutoring.** Watters' argument is that "personalized learning" has been the same promise for 100 years. If true, AI-tutoring claims should be held to a higher standard. If Watters is overreaching, the thesis should say why.
