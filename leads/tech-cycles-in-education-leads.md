## Primary sources to read

- **Cuban, Larry (1986). *Teachers and Machines: The Classroom Use of
  Technology Since 1920*.** Foundational. The book most subsequent edtech
  history defines itself in conversation with. Paywalled.
- **Cuban, Larry (2001). *Oversold and Underused: Computers in the
  Classroom*.** Post-PC follow-up. Paywalled.
- **Cuban, Larry (2018). *The Flight of a Butterfly or the Path of a
  Bullet?: Knowing When to Change Course in High-Tech Reform Efforts*.**
  Even more recent; specifically on tech-reform timing. Paywalled.
- **Watters, Audrey (2021). *Teaching Machines: The History of Personalized
  Learning*.** The recent canonical history of "personalized learning"
  claims. Paywalled.
- **Postman, Neil (1985). *Amusing Ourselves to Death*.** Media-ecology
  critique of television as a learning medium. Paywalled.
- **Postman, Neil (1992). *Technopoly*.** Broader critique. Paywalled.
- **Selwyn, Neil (2016). *Is Technology Good for Education?*.** Contemporary
  critical-edtech scholar; compact volume. Paywalled.
- **Saettler, Paul (1990). *The Evolution of American Educational
  Technology*.** Dense reference history. Paywalled.
- **Christensen, Clayton M., Horn, Michael B. & Johnson, Curtis W. (2008).
  *Disrupting Class*.** Disruption-theory case applied to K-12.

  of high-cost adoption with low practice change.

  Street* and predecessors actually demonstrated, and what they did not.

## Tensions to chase

- **The Cuban argument can be misread two ways.** First, as "tech never
  works in education" — Cuban is more careful than this; he documents
  specific reasons adoption fails. Second, as a *prediction* — Cuban
  describes a historical pattern, not a law of nature. The thread should
  engage both errors.
- **What is "the grammar of schooling"?** Tyack & Cuban's term. Important
  to define operationally before invoking it. Otherwise the argument is
  "schools resist change because schools resist change."
- **Generative AI's adoption pathway may be genuinely novel** in that it
  is reaching students directly, outside the institution. If so, the
  "schools absorb tech" record may not constrain it. But also: most
  studies of out-of-school learning effects on academic outcomes are weak.
- **Counter-examples matter.** The textbook, the blackboard, the
  calculator, the standardized test booklet — these *are* technologies
  that reshaped schooling. What made them succeed where film, radio, and
  TV failed?
- **The personalized-learning lineage is the most direct test case for AI
  tutoring.** Watters' argument is that "personalized learning" has been
  the same promise for 100 years. If true, AI-tutoring claims should be
  held to a higher standard. If Watters is overreaching, the thesis should
  say why.
- **MOOCs as the most recent prior wave** — Jordan 2014 is now in the
  ledger. The next move is either Pappano 2012 to pair the hype with the
  reality, or a more recent MOOC analysis to see if the steady state is
  different from Jordan's 18-month-old data.
