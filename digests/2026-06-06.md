## Tensions I noticed

One worth surfacing in detail: **the Illich Ch 6 reading complicates a
clean Illich-vs-thesis story in both directions.** Read Ch 1, Illich is
the strongest counter to the thesis on the ledger — schooling itself
is the problem; tools cannot solve a system problem; the proliferation
of educational hardware or software "in classroom or bedroom" will not
deliver universal education. Read Ch 6, Illich is the strongest
1971 antecedent of the positive case the thesis wants to make — a
peer-matching network operated by a computer; "tapes, retrieval
systems, programmed instruction" that "reduce the need for recourse to
human teachers of many skills"; "technology is available to develop
either independence and learning or bureaucracy and teaching." The
two chapters belong to the same author writing the same book in 1971.
The honest synthesis is that Illich is *for* technology in service of
learning and *against* technology in service of the institution; the
operative distinction for him is the *deployment*, not the tool.

This cuts in both directions for the thesis. *Supporting:* the
augmentation aim is recognisably in Illich's positive frame; Illich
gives the thesis a 50-year-old visionary anchor for the "technology
that enables previously impossible learning" idea. *Complicating:*
Illich's positive frame disposes of the teacher-as-funnel role — the
network operators are not pedagogues, and "network builders and
administrators would have to demonstrate genius at keeping themselves,
and others, out of people's way." That is the exact opposite of the
steer's teacher-agency reframing, which holds AI in service of the
teacher and rejects routing-around-the-teacher framings. The thesis
cannot have both: it cannot claim Illich Ch 6 as the visionary anchor
for its augmentation thread while also claiming that AI is in service
of the teacher's preserved relational core. Either it is in service of
the teacher (Dewey/Cuban) or it dissolves the teacher's role
(Illich Ch 6) — one or the other, not both. This is a real tension and
worth steering through.

The second tension is smaller and concerns Cuban 2025. The post's
selection includes Roberts admitting she does not read the per-student
AI feedback. That is partial substitution of teacher judgment, not
augmentation. Cuban 2024 was emphatic that AI advocates fail to
understand the complexity of teaching — particularly the relational
work. Cuban 2025 documents (without analytical comment) a teacher
delegating the relational-feedback work to the AI and getting time
back. The two halves of Cuban's own corpus on AI now contain the
warning *and* an example of the warned-against pattern. Worth marking
this rather than smoothing it.

## Thesis-fit check

Current line from the 2026-05-27 steer (operative direction):
*"AI specifically removes four constraints: the cost of expert
attention; the one-way nature of fixed content; the system-imposed
workload that forces teachers to teach to the middle of 30; the gap
between students and expert-level practice."*

This week's reading produces the most direct contemporary support for
constraints #2 and #3 the ledger has so far — Wallace's "instantly
produce simplified versions of readings on the causes of terrorism
for 14-year-olds who...have been learning English as a second
language" (#2), Ballaret's summer-recovery and the Indiana / Utah
parent-and-admin-email-automation cases (#3). Constraint #1 (cost of
expert attention) gets a smaller support — Roberts' faster-feedback
case — that is immediately bounded by her admission that the feedback
is not being checked by the teacher. Constraint #4 (gap to
expert-level practice) gets no new support this week.

The Illich-Ch-6 reading is harder to fit and more interesting. The
thesis-fit check question is whether Illich's positive frame supports
*this thesis* or a *different* thesis that the steer was explicit
about not adopting. My current best read: Illich supports the
augmentation aim *abstractly* but rejects the steer's teacher-with-AI
implementation. The thesis can use Illich for the visionary "tools can
enable kinds of learning previously impossible" claim and must engage
him (not absorb him) on the institutional question. I am not asking
for a re-steer; flagging it so the disagreement does not get smoothed
in subsequent runs.
