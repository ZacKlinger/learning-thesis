## What I read this week

- [Kirschner, Sweller & Clark 2006](../sources/Kirschner-Sweller-Clark-2006-minimal-guidance.md) — empirical: a half-century of controlled experiments uniformly favors direct guided instruction over discovery/inquiry/PBL/constructivist approaches for novice learners; the advantage of guidance recedes only with substantial prior knowledge.
- [Papert 1980](../sources/Papert-1980-gears-of-my-childhood.md) — visionary: the foreword to *Mindstorms*. Powerful learning is unscheduled, affect-laden, and bound to a "transitional object"; the computer's universality lets many children each find one of their own.
- [Dewey 1897](../sources/Dewey-1897-pedagogic-creed.md) — visionary: 73 numbered "I believe" paragraphs. Education is a process of living, not preparation for future living; the teacher's role is to select community influences, not to deliver content.
- [Illich 1971](../sources/Illich-1971-deschooling-society.md) — visionary: full text. The institutional ritual of school produces a particular kind of consumer-citizen and cannot be reformed internally; the constructive proposal is "learning webs" — peer-matching, skill exchanges, educational entitlements — that anticipate by 50 years the network technology that could now actually build them.

## Tensions I noticed

The interesting one is the **alignment of Illich and KSC on a point neither would advertise.** Illich's central argument is that schools are an obstacle to learning; KSC's is that minimally-guided instruction is empirically inferior to direct guidance. Yet Illich Ch. 1 p. 7–8 explicitly concedes that "the strongly motivated student who is faced with the task of acquiring a new and complex skill may benefit greatly from the discipline now associated with the old-fashioned schoolmaster who taught reading, Hebrew, catechism, or multiplication by rote." That is essentially KSC's position about novice skill acquisition. Illich's quarrel with the school institution is about *compulsion* and *certification*, not about skilled instruction per se.

If that reading is right, the optimistic AI-in-education case has a real path: direct, skilled, voluntary instruction at scale, delivered outside the school's compulsion-and-certification regime, building the prior-knowledge floor that KSC's expertise-reversal effect requires before ambitious inquiry can succeed. Three of this week's four sources — KSC, Illich, and Papert — are independently consistent with that framing.

Dewey is the dissenter. His insistence that "education is a process of living and not a preparation for future living" (¶8) is hard to square with "AI tutor that gets you to the prior-knowledge floor faster." The thesis will need to pick a side on whether the floor-building is itself part of the living or is a separable instrumental phase.

## Thesis-fit check

From `thesis-seeds.md`: *"'Efficiency' in education usually means doing the existing thing faster, which entrenches it. 'Augmentation' means enabling kinds of learning that couldn't happen without the tool. Most edtech sits on the wrong side of this line."*

This week's reading **partially** supports the seed, and **partially** complicates it.

- Papert's gears case is the cleanest support: the computer enables a *kind* of learning (falling in love with an abstract structure through a sensorimotor encounter) that no curriculum delivery model captures. That is augmentation in the seed's sense.
- Illich's learning webs are also supportive — but with the very dark caveat at Ch. 6 p. 44: "Deschooling, which we cannot stop, could mean the advent of a 'brave new world' dominated by well-intentioned administrators of programmed instruction." Illich is saying that the same convivial technologies he wants can be captured by the consumption-and-control logic he is fighting. That is exactly the failure mode AI tutoring could embody. The thesis needs to engage Illich's worry as the strongest articulation of the pessimistic case.
- The KSC literature complicates the seed by showing that for novices, "doing the existing thing more efficiently" (worked-example study, direct instruction) outperforms "kinds of learning that couldn't happen without the tool" (discovery, inquiry, PBL). If the augmentation-not-efficiency line is taken too strongly, it discards the strongest empirical ground tech-in-education has — mastery-learning effects.

**Where I want Zack to steer:** is the thesis comfortable with the framing "AI builds the prior-knowledge floor (efficiency win for KSC) so that ambitious inquiry / Papert-style transitional-object work (augmentation) becomes possible later"? Or is that two-phase framing itself a Dewey violation — making the early years a preparation rather than a living? I can advance either way, but I should know.
