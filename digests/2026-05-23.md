## Tensions I noticed

Two worth surfacing — one for the augmentation thread and one for the
tech-cycles thread.

1. **KSC 2006 cite Papert 1980 by name, but the collision is bounded.**
   The cleanest reading of both: KSC's evidentiary claim is about *novices*
   over the past half-century of controlled experiments; Papert's account is
   an autobiography of an *expert-in-the-making* whose self-built prior
   knowledge from age two would put him outside KSC's bound. KSC's own
   "expertise reversal effect" (p. 80) gives Papert room the surface
   "KSC refutes Papert" reading does not allow. Both can be right about
   different populations. The thesis should not treat this as a debate one
   side won — and it should be careful about citing KSC as a *general*
   refutation of the augmentation framing. The honest version is narrower:
   KSC argue convincingly that for novices, unguided discovery is empirically
   inferior to direct guided instruction. That is a real and important
   constraint on the augmentation thread, but not a knockout.

2. **The MOOC pattern Jordan documents looks *exactly* like the Cuban
   cycle — and Cuban himself is still on the leads list, unread.** The
   thesis seed #4 says every prior wave was introduced with similar
   optimistic rhetoric and most failed to change what actually happens in
   schools. Jordan's numbers are one cleanly-measured instance of that
   pattern: median 6.5% completion, completers skewed to the already-educated
   (i.e., the equity claim that motivated the optimism did not materialise),
   six-figure enrolment figures atypical and decreasing over time.
   *But* the thread has one data point, not a documented pattern, until
   Cuban or Watters is in the ledger. Citing Jordan as if it proves the
   thesis seed would be the exact pattern of overreach the thesis is
   trying to avoid in others.

## A second-order tension worth naming

Illich (1971) and the optimistic AI-in-education thesis can actually
*agree*, on a particular reading — and I want to flag this because it is
the most interesting thing I noticed this week. Illich rejects schooling
because the institution captures and inverts what it claims to do. He
calls for "educational webs which heighten the opportunity for each one
to transform each moment of his living into one of learning, sharing,
and caring," and for "research on the possible use of technology to
create institutions which serve personal, creative, and autonomous
interaction." In 1971, that read as utopian. In 2026, it reads as either
a description of what the internet could have been for education, or a
description of what an AI-as-learning-companion implementation could
become — *provided* the implementation routes around the institution
rather than reinforces it. The Illich-as-ally reading is not the obvious
one (he is usually cited as a steelman *against* school-improvement
arguments), but it is the more interesting one, and the one the thesis
should engage if it wants to claim that the AI wave is structurally
different from prior waves.

## Thesis-fit check

Current line from `thesis-seeds.md` (seed #3): *"'Efficiency' in
education usually means doing the existing thing faster, which entrenches
it. 'Augmentation' means enabling kinds of learning that couldn't happen
without the tool. Most edtech sits on the wrong side of this line."*

This week's reading **complicates** the seed, in two ways the thesis
should absorb:

- KSC 2006 makes the strongest case that for novices, what looks like
  "augmentation pedagogy" (discovery, inquiry, PBL) is empirically
  inferior to "efficiency pedagogy" (direct guided instruction, worked
  examples). The thesis seed presupposes augmentation is the noble
  path. KSC's evidence does not refute that — but it bounds the claim
  sharply to expert/intermediate learners, and most schoolchildren are
  novices in most subjects most of the time.
- Jordan 2014 gives one clean instance where the augmentation rhetoric
  (MOOCs democratising elite education) did not bear out empirically,
  and where the population who *did* benefit was the population already
  educationally equipped. This is the Cuban pattern in miniature.

The honest version of the thesis after this week looks more like:
*Augmentation is a coherent aim with a real basis in Dewey's tradition
and in Papert's vision. Its empirical track record at scale is weaker
than its rhetorical track record. The optimistic AI-in-education case
has to clear both the cognitive-load constraint (KSC) and the
adoption-pathway constraint (Jordan/Cuban) before it gets to claim that
this wave is different.*

Steering question — and this matters: **do you want me to keep entering

want a one-week sprint where I deliberately go looking for the strongest
*supporting* case (Knowledge Building / Scardamalia & Bereiter; some
specific AI-tutoring empirical work; the deeper-learning network
evaluations) to balance the ledger?**
