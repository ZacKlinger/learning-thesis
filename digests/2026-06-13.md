## Tensions I noticed

The biggest one is the structural challenge Watters 2018 poses to the
steer's *setup-not-foil* reframing of the tech-cycles thread.

The steer (2026-05-27, #3 of "three framings the agent must not use")
explicitly directs: *"Tech-cycles is setup, not foil. The historical
wave-by-wave pattern is the frame — the medium of learning has been
changing all along, AI is the next change. It is not the obstacle..."*

Watters 2018 sits inside the *foil* framing, not the setup framing. Her
position: the medium-change-rhetoric *is itself* the marketing claim,
repeated for a century. The actual change in each wave has been to the
political-economic infrastructure of schooling (corporate capture, data
extraction, proprietary lock-in), not to learning. On her account, the
thesis's "AI is the next change of medium" framing is the same move
Edison made in 1922 about film, the same move Skinner made in 1958 about
teaching machines, the same move the MOOC platforms made in 2012.

The steer is not self-evidently right against Watters. She has a century
of historical evidence behind the foil framing. The burden is on the
setup framing to specify *what is structurally different* about the
current change of medium. Cuban 2025 gives the thread its first piece of
that answer — bottom-up adoption by individual teachers on workload-
relief uses, structurally different from the top-down corporate-
procurement pipeline Watters spends most of her essay critiquing. But
that is one piece of an answer, not the whole answer. The bottom-up
adoption could itself be absorbed into the corporate pipeline (Microsoft /
OpenAI / Google EDU tier pricing is the test); the workload-relief uses
could turn into the algorithmic prediction Watters specifically warns
against (Roberts' "I do not look at the feedback" admission already
gestures at this).

I am not asking for a re-steer. I am surfacing that the steer's reframing
has work to do that this week's reading made visible. The thread is
keeping the reframing and entering Watters as the strongest 2018
challenge to it, with the burden the reframing must clear recorded
explicitly in the thread file.

The second tension is smaller and rhetorical. Watters identifies
"augment, not replace" as a century-old corporate marketing trope. The
thesis-seed v1 vocabulary ("augmentation, not efficiency") is sitting on
the historical-pattern side of that line. The 2026-05-27 steer's
reframing ("the truest forms of learning, whatever the research says
those forms actually are") is a partial response — it specifies the
*aim* rather than the marketing vocabulary — but the rhetorical proximity
remains. The augmentation thread now records this; the open question is
whether the vocabulary should be retired.

## Thesis-fit check

Current line from the 2026-05-27 steer (operative direction):

*"AI specifically removes four constraints: the cost of expert
attention; the one-way nature of fixed content; the system-imposed
workload that forces teachers to teach to the middle of 30; the gap
between students and expert-level practice."*

This week's reading does *not* directly support any of the four
constraints. Watters' 2018 case is that the rhetoric of constraint-removal
is itself the marketing trope. That is not a refutation of the four-
constraint frame — Watters is making a historical observation about
*claims*, not a substantive claim that the constraints don't exist —
but it is a warning. If the thesis cannot specify what makes its account
of "removing the cost of expert attention" different from a century of
"this product will save teachers time" pitches, the four-constraint
frame is rhetorically vulnerable in the same way Watters is critiquing.

have *no* contemporary empirical evidence on the ledger for #1 (cost of

Bereiter for #4). Three weeks of fetch attempts later, neither is on
